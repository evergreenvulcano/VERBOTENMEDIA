# The Body Without Coordinates

- **Section:** Writings
- **Type:** Essay
- **Year:** 2024

## Summary
On physical experience in tracked, measured, and datafied bodies.

## Draft

You enter the cave. You move through it by running your fingertips along the walls — *"det är så man färdas här, fingertopparna längtar efter andra texturer, handryggar raspar mot våtdov svalka."* This is how one travels here. The fingertips long for other textures. The backs of hands rasp against damp-cool surfaces. Orientation is haptic. The body knows where it is not by GPS coordinates but by what it touches.

There is a scene in a Verboten Media fiction document where three figures descend into a cave system that mirrors the narrator's meditation landscape. The body in this text is not tracked, not measured, not quantified. It is *felt* — present as sensation, as pressure, as the cracking of a neck that has stiffened from staring too long into darkness. The narrator tries to expand her visual field by *massaging her eyes from the inside*, pulling at them with consciousness itself, attempting to override the organ's natural function through sheer phenomenological will. The body here is not data. It is substrate. It is the thing that hurts when you try to see differently.

Set this against the body as it exists in contemporary technical infrastructure. Your phone knows your resting heart rate, your sleep cycles, your menstrual patterns, your step count, your blood oxygen saturation. Your fitness tracker constructs a coordinate system in which your body is always located — always positioned relative to yesterday's metrics, last week's average, the statistical norm for your age and sex. The body-with-coordinates is a body that can be optimized. And optimization implies a target state — a body you *should* be, measured against a model derived from aggregate data about bodies you will never meet.

The body without coordinates is the one that escapes this grid. It is the body that cracks its knuckles in a cave and hears the echo like *"en liten sten faller ned i ett större vatten"* — a small stone falling into a larger water. It is the body that knows things the tracker cannot measure: the weight of fourteen seconds spent looking at someone's photograph. The muscular tension of a message typed and deleted. The specific exhaustion of holding two contradictory emotions simultaneously without resolving them.

The datafied body and the felt body are not opposites. They coexist in the same flesh. But they answer to different authorities. The datafied body answers to the platform, the insurer, the employer's wellness program, the algorithm that adjusts your feed based on inferred stress levels. The felt body answers to nothing. It simply reports. And its reports — pain, pleasure, unease, recognition, the sudden warmth of being understood — arrive in formats no JSON field can capture.

The resistance available to those who notice is not to smash the tracker. It is to maintain the felt body's authority alongside the datafied one. To insist that the cracking of a neck in the dark is as real as a sleep score. To remember that the body's oldest coordinate system is touch, and touch requires proximity, and proximity cannot be optimized, only experienced.
