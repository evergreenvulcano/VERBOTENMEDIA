The Author-role as Social function is more interesting than “the Author as person”

Prelude: Words that carry memory

This essay is spawned from a phrase of a fragment that is a piece of paper: “The author as a channel to the collective consciousness for the one who wants to plant something there.”

It should be read as an externalized glimpse of internal directive documentation from Verboten Media: not marketing copy, not an explainer, not a personal statement, but a functional argument about what authorship does when language, memory, pressure, and form must be carried across unstable conditions.

The article keeps one principle intact: some words, images, and working forms carry memory because they have been used, failed with, returned to, and endured. Their value does not come from being immediately transparent. It comes from the fact that they have survived contact.

End of prelude

What an author does for the world

The author-role as social function is more interesting than “the author as person.”

The author as person has biography, voice, needs, trauma, style, ambition, weaknesses, desire. But the author-role as social function is something else: it is a place in society’s semantic circulation where certain kinds of pressure are allowed to pass through, gather, distort, condense, and be re-transmitted in forms that others can recognize without entirely understanding why.

An author, then, is not primarily “someone who expresses themselves.” An author is a human being who, under certain historical, bodily, and technical conditions, allows themselves to become a kind of passage-organ for language, experience, collective anxiety, social material, memory, shame, humor, fantasy, and form.

That sounds solemn, but it does not have to be mystical. It is quite practical.

Society constantly produces surplus that does not fit inside its official forms. It does not fit inside news, policy, family life, work, product descriptions, municipal protocols, therapeutic conversations, market language, academic categories, or everyday small talk. There is always something that cannot be said in the place where it arose.

The author-role is one of the functions capable of receiving that surplus.

Not because the author is more truthful than others. But because literature has a license to carry what does not yet have stable social status. A novel character is allowed to be a contradiction without immediately being diagnosed. A scene can carry guilt, comedy, desire, violence, triviality, and metaphysics at the same time. A fake newspaper article can say more about the shape of a society than a correct report. An absurd entrepreneur can reveal the real logic of an era better than an analyst.

So the author-role as social function is a formal asylum for insufficiently coded experience.

This is why the author is so often confused with the private person. The material passes through the body, so society assumes the body is the source. But the body is not only source. It is also filter, membrane, wound, receiver, amplifier, transit station.

In this sense, the poetic I can be understood as passage/wound: not merely a speaker, not merely a mask, but a site where material passes through a damaged or marked point of contact.

That does not mean the author is passive. On the contrary. Passage is an active discipline. To let something pass through without immediately reducing it to message requires enormous control. The most advanced form of control is sometimes not controlling too early.

Self-reference, then, is not “I only talk about myself”. It is: I use the place where the world has already left marks.

Here the author-role becomes socially unusual. Most institutions exist to stabilize meaning. The author often exists to keep meaning unstable long enough for it to assume a living form.

It is a social function.

Not necessarily a paid function. Not necessarily a recognized function. But an actual function.

The author delays acceleration.

Society often wants to know: what does this mean, who is responsible, what should be done, which category, which target group, which use, which risk, which conclusion?

The author can answer: no, it is not ready to become that yet.

This is not only artistic freedom. It is social maintenance work. A society that has no places where experience is allowed to remain in form before becoming decision, diagnosis, ideology, or product becomes semantically ill. It begins merely to process signals. It can no longer carry reality.

In that sense, the author is a kind of semantic maintenance worker.

But not like a bureaucrat. More like someone walking around inside the building of culture and noticing: it is leaking here, it smells like old water here, someone has painted over the mold here, children have begun sleeping in the industrial room here, the sign is official but the door leads nowhere.

That is why maintenance, logistics, boundary, and passage images work. They make the author-role less romantic and more material. The author is not a genius-fountain. The author is someone who notices pressure differences in the walls of the world.

This also makes “self-reference” less narcissistic than it sounds.

If the author as social function works through their own system, their own wounds, their own images, their own recurring forms, that does not automatically mean the work is locked inside the private person. It may mean that the private person happens to be the only place where certain social pressures have been sufficiently stored, endured, and shaped to be re-transmitted.

Self-reference, then, is not “I only talk about myself.”

It is:

I use the place where the world has already left marks.

That is a major difference.

The author as social function therefore does not carry only their “I.” It carries the forms through which the world has been able to damage, shape, seduce, deceive, feed, overload, and call upon that I. This is why extreme subjectivity can sometimes become more general than neutrality. Neutrality often only says what the system can already hear. Extreme subjectivity can say what the system does not yet know it has caused.

When writing no longer proves anything

This is a very important point for AI writing.

AI has no endured biographical body in the same sense. It has language, patterns, response, style, context, enormous imitation, some reasoning capacity, but not the human cost of having carried a word through time. AI can therefore generate strong form, but it needs another kind of anchoring in order not to become weightless.

That anchoring is not only “the memory of the project.” It is origin-artefacts, blueprints, wounds, old files, failed pitches, practical attempts, incorrect hypotheses, rediscovered papers, rough drawings, and source-words that have gathered functional pressure over time.

This is where the author-role in the age of AI changes.

The author is no longer only the one who produces the text. The author becomes the one who maintains the provenance of value.

Meaning: what is allowed to count as source? What is merely elegant language? Which term is older than the system? Which metaphor has been endured? Which image has body? Which output is merely impressive? Which output has contact?

The author does not become less important because AI can write. The author partly changes function. From solitary producer to provenance-bearer, pressure-holder, passage-maker, boundary-setter, feedback-worker, wound-witness, selection instance.

The false and the parasite

This is not a less romantic authorship. It is perhaps a more brutal authorship.

The author must be able to say:

This sounded good but lacks contact.

This is ugly but carries origin.

This is old but active.

Many real methods begin as socially inappropriate explanations.

This is new but parasitic.

This misunderstood the work but opened the right door.

This must not be polished.

This must be allowed to continue.

This is not a book yet, but without this the book becomes false.

As social function, then, the author is not only “creator.” The author becomes the one who protects a certain kind of relation between life, language, and form from being dissolved by the apparatuses of the present.

And there is a paradox here: the author needs society, but cannot obey society’s fastest demands for interpretation.

A failed pitch can therefore be almost perfect. It shows an early social contact where the form could not yet be carried by the situation. The idea fell flat. But that does not mean the idea was weak. It may mean the social function did not yet have the right medium. The proposed form may have been the wrong carrier. Or the pitch was too early. Or the blueprint was already a method, not a sales proposal.

Many real methods begin as socially inappropriate explanations.

The author is often the one who survives the failure of the first social transfer.

That, too, is a function.

Not throwing away what cannot yet be received.

Self-denial as failed communication

Here there is a social-philosophical core: all cultures need people who do not immediately convert failed communication into self-denial. Someone must be able to say: “It did not land, but that does not mean it was dead. It means its form had not yet found the right passage.”

That is the author-role.

And perhaps even more: the author is the one who maintains contact with the material’s future receivability.

Not the audience that exists now. But the audience, the form, the situation, the system, the book, the collaboration, in which the material can suddenly become readable.

That is why the author is a social function despite solitude.

The author sometimes works alone because the social form does not yet exist. Solitude is not necessarily withdrawal. It can be a temporary construction site for future sociality.

A blueprint can fall flat as a pitch and later reveal itself as a methodological core.

This does not mean AI “understood” the material in a magical sense, or that the first receiver failed. It means that a new social-technical situation arose in which old material could answer back.

The author-role as social function is then to have endured the gap between first failed transfer and later possible readability.

That is a great deal.

It is also why “the value of the word” is not empty rhetoric here. A word gains value through expenditure. Through someone carrying it, testing it, failing with it, returning to it, letting it change form, refusing to let assistants replace it with cheaper words, and finally discovering that it had more inner architecture than one remembered.

The author is the social function that endures such words until they can begin to act.

Not only write them.

Endure them.

What authorship must answer for now

The brutality lies in the fact that the author can no longer hide behind production.

When writing was scarcer, slower, more bound to hand, body, keyboard, paper, publisher, solitary workday, the author-role could more easily coincide with the act of bringing text into being. The one who wrote was an author because the text did not exist without that human being’s laborious producing. The form already carried a kind of proof: someone has sat here. Someone has chosen every sentence. Someone has spent time on this.

AI dissolves that old guarantee.

Text can now arise without a corresponding human expenditure. Form can be produced without anyone having endured its necessity. Tone can be simulated without carrying its biographical cost. Structure can be proposed without having passed through a life. The author therefore does not become less important, but more exposed. The author no longer only has to ask: can I write this? The question becomes: why should this be allowed to exist, what does it have contact with, what has it cost, what does it carry, what must it not replace?

It is a harder authorship because production no longer suffices as an excuse.

The old romantic author could say: here is my text, it is the fruit of my solitude. The new brutal author must say: here is a text, but I must be able to account for its contact, its source, its pressure, its right to enter the world. Not only legally. Not only academically. Ontologically.

That is provenance-bearing.

Provenance is not only “where does the file come from?” It is not only metadata, date, version, folder, history. It is a deeper question: where does this value come from? Which experience, which image, which damage, which failed communication, which blueprint, which stubborn repetition, which incomprehensibility, which social incapacity has carried this forward to the point where AI could help it assume form?

The author becomes the one who can distinguish between text that has merely arisen and text that has origin.

It is a brutal function because AI makes emergence cheaper and origin more important.

Holding what should not be flattened too soon

The pressure-holder is the next role.

Pressure is not idea. Ideas are easy. Pressure is what does not let go. What returns. What changes form but does not disappear. What cannot be summarized without loss. What first looks private, then comic, then methodological, then world-bearing.

AI is very good at relieving pressure. It wants to make things manageable. It wants to formulate, list, structure, suggest, package, make useful. This is an enormous help. But it can also let the pressure out too early. It can puncture it by understanding it too quickly.

The author as pressure-holder says: no, not yet. This must not become concept yet. This must not become plan yet. This must not become “content” yet. This must continue to be charged, unclear, comic, wounded, stupid, unreasonable, bodily, excessive, private, local, embarrassing, failed, because it is precisely there that its future form is still alive.

It is brutal because it sometimes means saying no to help.

Not no to AI. No to the part of help that reduces necessity.

Making a passage from wound to form

The passage-maker is the third role.

The author does not only produce text. The author builds passages between states. From blueprint to method. From wound to figure. From failed pitch to working architecture. From private image to socially legible form. From chaotic material to book. From book to system. From system back to prose.

Passage is not transport. It is transformation under risk.

What passes through is changed. A private paper becomes source. A source becomes method. A method becomes instruction. An instruction becomes prose. The prose becomes world. The world demands new documents. The documents begin to govern the prose in return. Then someone must guard the passage so that what was alive does not emerge as administration.

This is why boundary is so central. The author is not only the one who opens doors. The author builds permeable limits: places where material is allowed to change without dissolving.

The brutal part is that one must know what is allowed to pass and what must be stopped, even when one does not always know why.

Deciding what belongs and what does not

The boundary-setter is not the censor. The boundary-setter is the one who understands that everything does not receive the same status simply because it exists.

The great temptation of AI production is that everything becomes material. Everything can be summarized, integrated, expanded, cross-referenced, clustered, upgraded, turned into a prompt, turned into a method, turned into an artefact. It sounds generous, but it is also violent. If everything is allowed to pass into the system, the system loses contour. If everything becomes source, there are no sources. If everything becomes canon, there is no canon. If everything becomes possible book, there is no book.

The author in the age of AI must therefore be able to make an almost ascetic selection in the middle of abundance.

This belongs here.

That must wait.

That must never become text.

That is only beautiful noise.

That is embarrassing but carries origin.

That is brilliant but false.

That was written better by AI than I could have written it, but it has no contact.

That is ugly, but necessary.

The brutal part is that the selection no longer applies only to one’s own production. It applies to production’s entire cloud. Chat responses, agent runs, notes, errors, images, folders, documents, old blueprints, exports, intermediate versions. The author is no longer the one sitting before a blank page. The author becomes the one standing at a harbor while a thousand containers arrive, having to know which contain living seed, which contain machine parts, which contain poison, which contain props, which are merely empty but sound heavy when you knock on them.

Answering back without losing the thread

The feedback-worker is the fourth role.

AI can speak back, but the author must decide whether the answer has contact. That is not the same as deciding whether the answer is good. An AI response can be good but contactless. It can be elegant but secondary. It can be impressive but lack friction against the origin. And another answer can be skewed, almost wrong, but open exactly the right door.

The author becomes the one who maintains the feedback loop between living source and generated form.

It is no longer only “I write, the world answers.” It is “I write, the model answers, the sources answer, old documents answer, I correct the model, the model finds something I had forgotten, I correct it again, and suddenly a physical paper appears and shows that the method was already there.”

Feedback becomes a form of creative archaeology.

But it is brutal archaeology, because the excavation takes place in one’s own living workshop. One is not only digging in dead layers. One is digging in things that can still begin to bleed, begin to write, begin to demand a future.

Witnessing the wound without turning it into content

The wound-witness is perhaps the most uncomfortable role.

It is easy to romanticize wounds. One should not. A wound is not automatically art. Suffering does not legitimate form. But some forms could not have arisen without something having been endured. Then a witness is needed who neither turns the suffering into product nor allows the product to pretend to be frictionless.

AI can simulate wound-language. It can write beautifully about pain. It can recognize traumatic patterns in text. It can produce language that sounds deep. But it has not itself carried the word through the expenditure that made the word necessary.

The author as wound-witness says: there is a cost here that must not be exploited by the form, but must not be erased from the form either.

It is brutal because it requires a double refusal.

Refusal to sell the wound.

Refusal to let the wound be erased.

Between these lies literature’s difficult honor.

Choosing what is allowed to become real

The selection instance is finally the role to which all the others lead. Not selection as taste. Selection as responsibility for what is allowed to become real inside the project.

AI can offer a hundred continuations. The author must know which continuation is not only possible, but permitted by the work’s deeper contact. Not “permitted” as moral policing. Permitted as resonance. Permitted by the blueprint. Permitted by the boundary. Permitted by the word that has been endured.

This is where the author becomes more brutal than romantic.

The romantic image is that the author feels inspiration and writes. The brutal image is that the author stands between infinite generativity and a very small number of truly valid passages. The work is not to confuse generative richness with artistic permission.

AI can create an ocean. The author must know which wave actually comes from the depth.

And the open sea here is that the author-role may be returning to something older than modern “content production.” Something closer to seer, editor, priest, technician, archivist, gatekeeper, craftsperson, witness, liar, clown, and cartographer in the same body.

Not because the author is more sacred.

But because language has once again become dangerous in a more material way.

When text production is industrialized by models, form becomes cheap. When form becomes cheap, origin becomes expensive. When origin becomes expensive, the one who can carry origin becomes socially important in a new way.

It is not certain society will recognize this. On the contrary. The market will often want cheap form. Institutions will want controllable form. Platforms will want circulatable form. AI systems will want optimizable form.

The author must then stand for something much less comfortable:

non-interchangeable form.

Not because the words cannot be exchanged. Words can always be exchanged.

But because certain relations cannot be exchanged without the whole thing becoming false.

A source-word cannot simply be replaced by a smoother term.

A lived figure cannot simply be replaced by a framework.

A boundary-form cannot simply be replaced by governance.

A recurring inner character cannot simply be replaced by an agent persona.

A story-world cannot simply be replaced by “creative universe.”

The death of ... what, exactly? 

These substitutions can be comprehensible, sometimes even useful for brief moments. But if they become primary, something has died.

The author’s brutal function is to notice such deaths early.

This is also why this is not less romantic.

The romance remains, but it changes temperature. It is not the purple halo around the genius. It is rather the hard loyalty to an origin almost no one else can yet see.

Romance as endurance.

Romance as sanitation.

Romance as refusal to let the easily comprehensible replace the real.

Romance as carrying a rough mark, phrase, drawing, or failed explanation through months of methodological forgetting until it becomes visible again as blueprint.

It is a brutal romance.

And it has a social function precisely because society itself no longer knows how to distinguish between generated form and endured form.

The author can become one of the places where this distinction is still practiced.

Not because the author is always right. But because the author is the one who has carried the consequence of being wrong, returning, trying, failing, preserving, correcting, and continuing.

In a world where everything can be written, someone must still be able to say:

This is not only written.

This has passed through.

Why language still matters

What is being formulated here is not an incomprehensible hangup. It is a very precise existential problem for language after generative AI.

Letting go of language can be rest for the individual, while at the same time leaving the field open for systems that do not rest.

Nor is it merely “Western language fetishism against Eastern teachings of presence.” That conflict would be too simple. What is being attacked is something more specific: the historical indecency of urging human beings to withdraw from linguistic articulation precisely when language’s responsibility, discernment, friction, provenance, and human consequence are being subjected to technical mass production.

Meditation itself is not the enemy. The enemy is the situation in which linguistic retreat begins to look like wisdom while machines, platforms, institutions, and models increase their capacity to speak on behalf of the human being.

Then “be present, let go of your thoughts, let the words pass” is not necessarily liberation. It can become abdication. Not productive abandonment, where a form is handed over so it can continue to live, but a poorer abdication: the human being renounces the linguistic faculty needed to notice what is happening to her.

That is where the objection has its core.

Stones need no tradition of thought in order to be stones in the moonlight. Human beings need traditions of thought because they do not merely exist. They interpret that they exist. They suffer from knowing that they exist. They are surrounded by others who also interpret, demand, remember, damage, forgive, organize, mislead, explain, and name. Language is not a cosmetic layer on top of presence. It is part of the human way of staying awake inside its own black box.

This is why language-reduction becomes different in the age of AI.

In the past, one could say: the world is over-articulated, civilization suffocates us with concepts, return to silence, body, breath, simplicity. That had force because the threat was often the overcoding of the social machine: religion, bureaucracy, class, morality, industry, mass media, the bourgeois salon, nationalist phrases, psychologizing shame.

But now there is a new situation: language is not only an oppressive structure. Language is also a human capacity being externalized, automated, packaged, and economized on an enormous scale.

Then the same advice can change political temperature.

The old question was often: can I express the inner? The new question is: which expressions may pass through me without betraying the inner, the historical, the social, the wounded, the not-yet-finished?

Letting go of language can be rest for the individual, while at the same time leaving the field open for systems that do not rest.

Here lies the existential.

The author in the age of AI does not stand only before emptiness, death, love, meaninglessness, the outsider position, spleen, the fragility of the body, the lie of society. All of that remains. But the author also stands before a new colonization: not only of work, image, knowledge, or attention, but of the very capacity to formulate a relation to the world.

When language production becomes technically infinite, linguistic responsibility does not become less important. It becomes almost unbearably important.

The author’s position becomes existential because she no longer has to choose only words against silence. She must choose words against a global apparatus capable of producing reasonable words without having stood inside the existential cost of them.

That is why the formulation about “a very small number of truly valid passages” is central. The old question was often: can I express the inner? The new question is: which expressions may pass through me without betraying the inner, the historical, the social, the wounded, the not-yet-finished?

It is harder.

And here the spleen intuition becomes relevant. Spleen was not only melancholy. It was a sensory-intellectual disturbance in the relation between the individual, modernity, and the accelerated signs of the city. A black filter, a capacity for estrangement, a way of not being fully assimilated by the surfaces of modern experience. The poet did not become important because the poet was sad. The poet became important because sadness, estrangement, the hole, could give society another readability of itself.

It is the same form in a new technical environment.

The spleen of the AI age is perhaps not only melancholy. It is feeling how language becomes too easy. How everything can be said. How every pain quickly receives beautiful form. How every origin concept can become “framework.” How every wound-line can become a productive reflection. How every unarticulated image can be picked up by the machine and returned with improved syntax but lowered existential gravity.

The new spleen is not wordlessness.

It is revulsion before frictionless articulation.

This is why the author cannot settle for presence. Presence is not wrong. But presence without linguistic responsibility can become a private shelter while the public sphere, the market, and technology take over the power of formulation.

Language, then, is not the opposite of presence.

Language is a way of giving presence enough walls not to disappear.

This is a hard thought. It runs against much contemporary self-help, where language is often presented as an overlay: stories you carry, thoughts you can release, identifications you can see through. There is truth there. Human beings can be trapped by linguistic compulsions. But in the language-reductionism of the AI age there is another danger: that human beings learn to distrust their own capacity for articulation while the systems around them become more and more articulation-powerful.

That is a bad trade.

You get personal calm. The system gets language.

That is where the anger in this position lies.

And then the author’s seclusion is not merely ridiculous withdrawal from the world. It can be an attempt to keep a linguistic faculty intact when the surrounding world wants to make it either therapeutically dissolved or technically outsourced.

It is risky, of course. Seclusion can become narcissism. Self-reference can become enclosure. The value of the word can become a private cult. That is why social function is needed. The author must not simply sit and worship her inner hole. But nor must she allow society to fill the hole with ready-made words.

The author must preserve the form of the hole long enough for it to become passage.

This is exactly where “wound-witness” becomes important. Not the wound as merit. Not suffering as legitimation. But the wound as evidence that there is an opening where the world has actually passed through the body. If language comes out of that opening, and if it is shaped with sufficient precision, it can become more general than neutral discourse. It can carry a social truth precisely because it does not try to begin as socially accepted truth.

What it costs to say something true

This is also where certain literary traditions become an important resonant ground. Not as homogeneous inheritance, but as examples of how existential situation, bodily experience, social position, linguistic stubbornness, and formal precision can produce texts that do not merely “express” marginality but restructure what the center is able to recognize as reality.

That is what strong literature does: it shifts the criteria of reality.

It does not merely say: here is my experience.

It says: if my experience is true, your image of the world must change.

AI can help formulate such things. But AI can also produce the simulacrum of such a thing. That is why the author becomes an existential selection instance. She must feel the difference between text that sounds like a shift in the criteria of reality and text that actually carries the pressure of such a shift.

This cannot be fully automated.

Not because the human being is magical, but because the consequence of incorrect formulation lands in the human being.

The machine can be wrong without being humiliated, changed, damaged, socially misunderstood, or forced to carry its word through time. The author cannot. The author pays in expenditure. Therefore the author’s words carry another kind of risk. Not always higher quality, but a different risk profile.

And risk is part of value.

This is why “existential” today cannot mean only inner authenticity. It must also mean the defense of the human capacities that make authenticity articulable, testable, transmissible, and responsible.

When language is pressured by technical colonization, language-capacity is not only a means of expression. It becomes an existential infrastructure.

To think clearly, to name slowly, to refuse replacement words, to hold fast to the difference between an endured word and its convenient substitute — this can sound small, almost ridiculous. But at the micro level, this is exactly how linguistic colonization either happens or is stopped.

Colonization does not have to begin with censorship. It can begin with convenient translation.

“We’ll call this a framework.”

“We’ll call this governance.”

“We’ll call this content.”

“We’ll call this prompt engineering.”

“We’ll call this ideation.”

Each such translation can be locally useful. But if it replaces the origin word, an entire experiential geology is lost. Then it is not only a word that has been exchanged. A line of contact has been severed.

The author as social function is the one who notices the severing.

That is why the hangup is not incomprehensible. It is a sensor for severance.

It registers when the word no longer carries its expenditure. When a term becomes too light. When a system gives a comprehensible label but asks to be paid in resonance. When mindfulness offers calm but perhaps also a dangerous retreat from the struggle that must now be fought at the level of language.

Again: not as a rejection of stillness. Stillness may be needed in order to hear the word. But if stillness becomes idealized wordlessness in a time when technology mass-produces language, then stillness becomes politically and existentially ambiguous.

The real alternative is perhaps not more language or less language.

It is more responsible contact between word and existence.

Not to explain away the mystery, but to keep the human being near it without surrendering her capacity for orientation.

And here we return to the author.

The author is not important because she always writes best. AI will often write faster, cleaner, more variably, sometimes more beautifully. The author is important because she can say: this language has or lacks existential contact.

She can be wrong. But she can also carry the consequence of being wrong.

That is socially necessary.

Because if no one carries that consequence, only circulation remains. Text feeding text. Advice feeding advice. Presence sold against stress. Efficiency sold against overload. Criticism summarized. Wounds stylized. Language simulating responsibility.

The author, in the brutal sense, stands there and says:

No. Certain words must be endured before they may be used.

Certain images must be allowed to remain until they themselves reveal their scale.

Certain concepts are not labels but living storage-forms for contact with the world.

Certain silences are not mindfulness but repression.

Certain forms of calm are capitulation.

And certain forms of seclusion are not escape, but a workshop where a future social function cannot yet be understood.

Consciousness, language, and what we pass on

This is where the sentence lands: “I have an incomprehensible hangup, just like life itself in the form of consciousness as knowledge transmission and byproduct.”

Yes. Consciousness is perhaps precisely an incomprehensible hangup that evolution has not managed to reduce away. A surplus that does not only solve problems, but also carries traces, regret, possibilities, names, wounds, forms, ancestors, readings, failed pitches, drawings, notes in corners.

The human being is not merely presence.

The human being is presence that needs to be transmitted without becoming false.

That is why language is existential.

And that is why the author, in the open sea of the AI age, is not a decorative remnant from the era of print culture.

The author is one of the last figures who can insist that language is not only production, but the responsible transmission of existential contact through time.

// Martin
