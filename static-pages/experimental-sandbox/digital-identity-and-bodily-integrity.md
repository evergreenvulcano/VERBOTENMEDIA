# Digital Identity and Bodily Integrity

- **Section:** Experimental Sandbox
- **Status:** Fragment

## Summary
Preliminary notes on divergence and conflict between embodiment and digital representation.

## Draft
She tries to expand her visual field by massaging her eyes from the inside — pulling at them with consciousness itself, attempting to override the organ's natural function through sheer phenomenological will. The narrator in the cave is doing something that no digital interface can replicate: she is trying to change the body's hardware through software — through intention, through concentration, through the pressure of wanting to see more than the eyes are designed to reveal.

This is where digital identity and bodily integrity diverge most violently and most revealingly. The digital self can be edited with precision. Change your name, your photo, your bio, your pronouns, your location, your relationship status — the platform updates instantly, propagates globally, and the previous version of you is either archived or annihilated depending on the platform's data retention policy. The body cannot be edited this way. The body is the thing that cracks its knuckles and hears the echo — "en liten sten faller ned i ett större vatten." The body is what stiffens when you sit too long. The body is what hurts when you try to see differently.

The novel places bodies under extraordinary pressure. Kat Persson runs ultramarathons — "Ett ultra-lopp är som att äta sig igenom flera livstider" — and in doing so produces a form of knowledge that language cannot generate. The body, pushed past its comfort threshold and then past its pain threshold and then past the point where thresholds cease to be meaningful categories, discovers something about duration, about persistence, about the relationship between identity and exhaustion that no essay can convey. The body knows. It cannot say what it knows. That is its integrity — its refusal to be fully translated into information.

Bill Mackenberry kills a deer with his bare hands. This is the most visceral scene in the novel — a body acting at the level of animal capability, without interface, without mediation, without the protective layer of technology that normally stands between human intention and physical consequence. The act is horrifying and clarifying in equal measure. It strips the body back to its primary function: a thing that can exert force on the world through direct contact. No screen. No proxy. No avatar. Just hands, and the deer, and the irreversible consequence of contact.

The probe's preliminary notes map the territory between these extremes — the edited digital identity and the uneditable body, the avatar that updates instantly and the flesh that changes only through time, labor, or violence. The institutional conflicts are already visible: the biometric passport that fixes your face as your identity while the platform encourages you to curate a face that looks nothing like your passport photo. The fitness tracker that constructs a coordinate system for your body — always positioned relative to yesterday's metrics, the statistical norm for your age and sex — while the body itself operates in a coordinate system of sensation, discomfort, and craving that no JSON field can capture.

The fragment does not resolve the relationship. It notes the divergence, the contradiction, the institutional friction. It leaves a space for the question the cave narrator is really asking: what happens when you try to change the body using only the mind? And the counter-question: what happens when the platform changes your identity without consulting your body at all?


### Praktikantens Arbetsnoteringar
#### Edited identity, persistent body
The Clone's body is Arthur's body — same DNA, same physical structure, same face, same hands. But the Clone's identity is not Arthur's identity: one memory has been removed, one trauma excised via SHA2 modification (Chapter 11). The body's integrity is maintained while the identity is edited. This is the novel's sharpest formulation of the digital identity problem: if identity is data, and data can be edited without altering the body, then bodily integrity and identity integrity are independent variables. You can have a complete body with an incomplete self, or — as Arthur in Sandträsk demonstrates — a deteriorating body with an unedited, crushingly complete self. "Riggar" (Chapter 1) — Mary's father's concept of technology as cage around the body — extends the problem architecturally. The rig is not a prosthesis; it is a containment structure. The body does not extend into the technology; the technology encloses the body. Digital identity, in this framework, is not something the body produces; it is something the body is enclosed by. Nova's anti-screenshot app (Chapter 1) addresses the problem from the defensive side: digital identity protected from unauthorized capture, the body's image (via Snapchat) shielded from being frozen into a permanent digital artifact. And Dawkin's DNA monologue — "DNA är inte svartvitt, det är dubbeltupp" (Chapter 13, p. 157) — undermines the binary entirely: biology is not digital, not ones and zeros, but a spiral. The double helix is not a binary code. It is a continuous structure that folds, twists, and replicates without ever reducing to a simple on/off switch. Bodily integrity, the novel insists, is analog. Digital identity is an approximation — useful, powerful, but never identical to the body it claims to represent.
