# The Attention Audit

- **Section:** Experimental Sandbox
- **Status:** Active · Conceptual Prototype

## Summary
A long-running prototype treating attention as a cultural object across economics, politics, and aesthetics.

## Draft
Borderland operates on noll procent algoritm — zero percent algorithm. No schedule is pushed. No recommendation engine curates your path. No notification system interrupts your attention to redirect it toward something the platform has calculated you are likely to engage with. You walk through the festival's physical space and encounter what you encounter. The experience is disorienting, demanding, and structurally unlike anything the digital environment permits.

The Attention Audit takes this principle and applies it as a diagnostic method to daily life outside the festival. It treats attentional experience as an object of cultural study — not a wellness metric, not a productivity framework, not a self-help intervention, but a field observation of how attention actually behaves when it is simultaneously an economic resource, a political instrument, and an aesthetic problem.

The audit's method is structured self-observation. Not meditation — meditation aims to change attentional behavior. The audit aims only to document it. What captured your attention in the last hour? Not what you chose to attend to — what captured you. The distinction matters because capture implies an external agent: something reached into your attentional field and redirected it without your explicit consent. The notification chime. The subject line visible in the lock screen. The movement in the peripheral vision that turned out to be an ad auto-playing in a tab you forgot was open.

The novel provides the audit's foundational insight through the publisher's own attentional pathology. He is a man whose attention has been trained by twenty years of content-flow to operate at a frequency optimized for scanning rather than inhabiting. He processes satellite domains — Design, Tech, Game Theory, Ethics, Philosophy, Economics, Culture Engineering, Bob Dylan, Physiognomy, Neuroplasticity, Esoterica, Timelines, Physics — at the speed of administration, not the speed of thought. His attention touches everything and rests on nothing. This is not a personal failure. It is a professional adaptation to an environment that rewards broad monitoring and punishes deep engagement.

Lillfrugan's attention operates differently. She watches. She waits. She delivers seven-word sentences that contain more diagnostic precision than his entire satellite array. "Det ser ut som att du tillber internet" is the product of sustained, patient, unmediated attention — the kind that the algorithmic environment cannot produce because it has no engagement metric, no click, no scroll, no shareable output. Her attention is invisible to the infrastructure. That is what makes it powerful.

The audit maps three economies of attention:

The captured economy. Attention as a resource extracted by platforms through design patterns that exploit neurological vulnerabilities. The swipe, the infinite scroll, the variable-ratio reinforcement schedule of the notification system. This economy is well-documented. The audit adds granularity: not "attention is captured" but "at 14:23 my attention was captured by a subject line for eleven seconds, during which I lost the thread of the paragraph I was writing, and the cost of recapture was four minutes and a subtle mood shift I did not notice until later."

The directed economy. Attention deliberately allocated by the subject toward a chosen object. Reading. Writing. Listening with intent. The experience of directing attention against the current of capture — the specific muscular effort of not checking the phone, not opening the tab, not responding to the chime.

The residual economy. The attention that remains after capture and direction have been accounted for. The drift. The idle gaze. The moment between tasks when the mind is not captured and not directed but simply present — and the increasingly rare experience of discovering what attention does when it is left alone.

The audit does not prescribe. It records. It asks: where was your attention? Who put it there? What did it cost to move it? And — the question Borderland's zero-percent algorithm makes possible — what would your attention do if nothing was trying to capture it?

### Praktikantens Arbetsnoteringar
#### Default world
"Noll procent algoritm" at Borderland (Chapter 12) is the audit result when algorithmic attention capture reaches zero: attention disperses, follows the body, attaches to fire and sound and proximity rather than to feed position and engagement metrics. The result is not utopia — it is disorientation. Without algorithms, attention must be self-directed, which is harder than it sounds and more exhausting than anyone anticipated. The festival audits attention by removing the infrastructure that normally manages it, and the audit reveals that most people have forgotten how to direct their own attention without assistance. Dawkin's "skrytsociosfären" (Chapter 10, pp. 99–100) audits attention at the professional level: LinkedIn as a platform where attention is both currency and performance, where every post is simultaneously a message and a bid for visibility. Mary as PR strategist (Chapter 7) audits attention at the institutional level: managing attention for Bob Dylan, Zlatan, Avril Lavigne, directing public perception through strategic communication — an attention audit performed professionally, for clients, with measurable outcomes. The 100-copy print run audits attention at the minimum viable level: one hundred copies directed to one hundred readers, attention concentrated to its irreducible minimum. And the trial (Chapter 16) audits attention at the spectacle level: Jordan Larsson testifying on television, the nation watching a scientist defend his right to destroy a color. The novel audits attention at every scale — personal (Mary's evaluation model), professional (PR), cultural (LinkedIn), subcultural (Borderland), legal (the trial) — and finds, at every scale, that attention is the resource everyone wants to manage and no one fully understands.
