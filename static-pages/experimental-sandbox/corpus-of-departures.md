# Corpus of Departures

- **Section:** Experimental Sandbox
- **Status:** Fragment · In Progress

## Summary
A corpus experiment on the textual residue of digital exits.

## Draft
The abdication interview was filed under Content > Writings, alongside everything else that was always already inside the novel. No reader was told it was fiction. No reader needed to be told. This is the formal structure of every digital departure: a text that does not announce its own genre, encountered by readers who must decide for themselves whether they are witnessing documentation or performance.

This probe collects, categorizes, and analyzes the textual residue of digital exits as a genre — a recognizable literary form with its own conventions, its own rhetorical strategies, its own cultural symptomatology.

The farewell post has a grammar. It typically opens with a temporal marker ("After X years..."), proceeds through a gratitude sequence (naming specific communities, collaborators, or experiences), performs a pivot toward future-orientation ("I'm excited about what comes next..."), and closes with a benediction directed at the community being left behind. The grammar is so consistent that departures which violate it — the abrupt deletion, the account that simply goes dark, the profile that is replaced overnight by a single cryptic sentence — register as transgressions. They are read as anger, breakdown, or crisis precisely because they refuse the expected form.

The novel generates departures at multiple scales. Arthur's nine-month isolation in Sandträsk is a departure from social existence so complete it nearly becomes permanent. Försäkringsmannen's relocation to Trieste is a departure that converts itself into an alternative mode of habitation — refusing translation technology, reading newspapers in a language he doesn't fully understand, enjoying the incomprehension. The publisher's abdication is the departure that performs departure without actually departing — the announcement of leaving as proof that the thing being left can survive without you.

And Noelle's disappearance in the cave: "utan en skymt av Noelle." No farewell post. No gratitude sequence. No future-orientation. Just presence, and then the absence of presence, and the body's inventory of what remains — stiffened neck, dried eyes, the hollow expansion of a heart that feels like thin metal expanding.

The corpus classifies departures along three axes:

Voluntary vs. involuntary. The deactivated account vs. the banned account. The retirement post vs. the platform shutdown notice. The distinction matters because voluntary departure carries the charge of agency — the person chose to leave — while involuntary departure carries the charge of violence. But the line between them is thinner than it appears. The person who deactivates their account after years of algorithmic manipulation — did they choose freely, or did the infrastructure make continued presence unbearable?

Announced vs. silent. The farewell post vs. the sudden disappearance. The announcement converts departure into a communicative act — one last message, one last performance, one last request for witness. The silent departure refuses this. It withdraws the self from the communicative field without explanation, leaving behind the specific unease of a conversation partner who realizes, days or weeks later, that the other person is simply gone.

Reversible vs. irreversible. The deactivated account can be reactivated. The deleted account cannot. The dead person's profile persists as a memorial page, which is the most irreversible departure of all — not the departure of the person, but the departure of departure itself. The profile remains. The person does not return. The departure never completes.


### Praktikantens Arbetsnoteringar
#### Departure as source-of-gravity
Skylar "Blue" — Arthur's daughter — dies of cancer in Chapter 6, and her death is the founding departure that drives every subsequent action in Arthur's thread. The blue, the clone, the gas, the trial: all of these are consequences of Skylar's departure, radiating outward from a hospital bed in concentric circles of grief, obsession, and creative destruction. The novel does not sentimentalize this. It does not describe the death in detail. It mentions it, places it, and then shows its effects — an approach that treats departure not as an event but as a gravitational source, something that warps the space around it long after it has occurred. Arthur's trial at Högsta domstolen (Chapter 16) is a departure from scientific legitimacy — a self-exile from the collective, from institutional affiliation, from the social contract that says a scientist creates knowledge rather than destroys it. Mary's question to Dawkin in the casino boat stairwell — "Är det en psykopomp?" (Chapter 13, p. 169) — names the novel's theology of departure: psychopomps are guides through the transition, death-angels at the threshold, figures who lead the departing soul from one state to another. The cards themselves, grounded in the novel, become a corpus of departures — each one leading the reader toward a book they may never read, guiding attention toward an artifact that exists in only one hundred copies, most of them already departed into private collections and unvisited shelves. And in the hospital tent at Borderland (Chapter 12, pp. 142–151), someone dies at a festival designed for life — a literal departure inside a temporary city built to celebrate presence. The corpus grows.
