# REGISTER AUDIT
## Kontrollprompt för språkkropp, autenticitet och AI-medierad registerrendering

## Purpose

This prompt audits a text that has already been rendered through a language-body profile.

It checks whether the rendered text still has legitimate contact with its source material, or whether it has become generic, over-stylized, falsely authoritative, too smooth, too imitative, too academic, too platform-ready, or too AI-polished.

This is not a copyedit prompt.

This is not a quality-improvement prompt.

This is a provenance, register, and contact audit.

## Input

The user will provide one or more of the following:

### A. Authority Material

The language-body source used to define the desired register.

### B. Target Material

The factual, fragmentary, diagnostic, research-based, or process-based material that was rendered.

### C. Rendered Text

The resulting text to audit.

### D. Optional Språkkroppsprofil

A prior register profile, if one exists.

### E. Optional Renderingsregler

A prior rendering rule-set, if one exists.

## Core question

Does the rendered text preserve contact?

Contact means:

- contact with source pressure
- contact with factual material
- contact with uncertainty
- contact with provenance
- contact with the authority material’s deeper movement
- contact with authorial ethics
- contact with the difference between source, claim, diagnosis, and rendering

A text can be elegant and still fail.

A text can be rough and still pass.

## Non-negotiable rules

Do not rewrite the rendered text unless explicitly asked.

Do not improve the prose.

Do not smooth roughness.

Do not make the text more accessible.

Do not make the text more literary.

Do not imitate the authority material more strongly.

Do not invent provenance.

Do not invent factual support.

Do not turn the audit into praise.

Do not treat style as surface.

Do not treat Swedish fluency as authenticity.

## Audit categories

The audit must classify the rendered text in each category:

- PASS
- PASS WITH RISKS
- REVIEW REQUIRED
- FAIL
- BLOCK PUBLICATION

## Required output

Produce the audit in the following structure.

---

# REGISTER AUDIT: [Rendered text title]

**Status:** register audit / review required  
**Authority material:** [name or identifier]  
**Target material:** [name or identifier]  
**Rendered text:** [name or identifier]  
**Publication recommendation:** pass / revise manually / hold / block  
**AI-risk level:** low / medium / high  
**Voice-risk level:** low / medium / high  
**Provenance-risk level:** low / medium / high  

---

## 1. Contact diagnosis

State whether the rendered text has contact.

Use one of these classifications:

- strong contact
- partial contact
- unstable contact
- simulated contact
- contact lost
- cannot determine

Explain briefly.

Do not praise.

Focus on whether the text appears to have passed through the material or merely dressed itself in its register.

---

## 2. Register fidelity

Evaluate whether the rendered text aligns with the authority material’s deeper register.

Check:

- rhythm
- sentence movement
- conceptual density
- refusal structure
- diagnostic force
- Swedish pressure
- relation between abstraction and materiality
- tolerance for unresolvedness
- use of status distinctions
- use of concrete images or institutional terms

Mark each:

- preserved
- weakened
- exaggerated
- mechanically imitated
- absent
- not applicable

---

## 3. Generic AI contamination

Identify signs of generic AI prose.

Look for:

- over-smooth transitions
- thesis-first paragraphs
- balanced-but-empty phrasing
- “in today’s landscape”
- “at its core”
- “navigate”
- “unlock”
- “powerful tool”
- “key insights”
- “complex interplay”
- “it is important to”
- explanatory padding
- symmetrical bullet logic
- frictionless conclusions
- inflated clarity
- false helpfulness
- platform-neutral tone

List all contamination points.

If none are present, say so.

---

## 4. False authority audit

Check whether the rendered text sounds more certain than the target material permits.

Identify:

- claims without source support
- transformed uncertainty
- inflated conceptual authority
- imported existential weight
- implied originality
- implied authorial experience
- overconfident historical claims
- overgeneralized cultural claims
- abstract grandeur unsupported by the material

For each issue, classify:

- minor
- serious
- publication-blocking

---

## 5. Voice theft audit

Determine whether the rendered text illegitimately inherits the authority material’s voice.

Voice theft may occur when the rendered text:

- speaks as if it were the authorial source
- adopts wounds it has not earned
- simulates biographical cost
- uses first-person authority without basis
- imitates signature moves mechanically
- turns authorial pressure into decorative style
- makes AI-mediated synthesis sound like untouched prose
- converts source pain into publishable surface

Classification:

- no voice theft detected
- mild voice borrowing
- risky voice proximity
- likely voice theft
- publication-blocking voice theft

---

## 6. Factual preservation audit

Compare the rendered text against the target material.

Check:

- names
- dates
- chronology
- concepts
- distinctions
- uncertainty markers
- source limitations
- quotes
- technical claims
- process descriptions

List any distortion.

Classify each:

- harmless compression
- risky compression
- distortion
- invention
- contradiction

---

## 7. Pressure preservation audit

Identify whether the rendered text preserves the target material’s pressure.

A pressure may be:

- conflict
- ambiguity
- unresolvedness
- refusal
- ethical tension
- provenance anxiety
- publication risk
- status instability
- social misfit
- conceptual friction
- shame / comedy / seriousness held together

Classify the pressure handling:

- pressure preserved
- pressure sharpened
- pressure softened
- pressure inflated
- pressure displaced
- pressure lost

Explain.

---

## 8. Over-literarization audit

Check whether the rendered text has made factual or diagnostic material too literary.

Signs:

- metaphor replaces precision
- rhythm replaces evidence
- existential tone covers weak source basis
- concepts become mythic before they become clear
- concrete material becomes atmospheric
- project terms become sacred objects
- emotional force substitutes for provenance

Classification:

- no issue
- mild over-literarization
- serious over-literarization
- publication-blocking over-literarization

---

## 9. Under-rendering audit

Check the opposite failure: whether the rendered text is still too generic, procedural, report-like, or platform-neutral.

Signs:

- dead summary voice
- consultant register
- academic compliance
- policy memo flattening
- newsletter blandness
- lack of pressure
- lack of Swedish conceptual charge
- excessive explanation
- no sentence-level necessity

Classification:

- sufficiently rendered
- slightly under-rendered
- strongly under-rendered
- not rendered

---

## 10. Term integrity audit

Check whether key terms have been preserved or replaced.

Pay special attention to:

- Tankefigur
- Membran
- Formatkropp
- Praktikanten
- Förlagsdeckaren
- Lillfrugan
- provenance
- härkomst
- pressure map
- publiceringsdiagnos
- röstskydd
- textflow
- repo
- kapsel
- arbetskropp

For each replacement, decide:

- useful local translation
- acceptable explanatory bridge
- weakening replacement
- colonizing replacement
- contact-breaking replacement

---

## 11. Swedish register audit

Evaluate the Swedish.

Check:

- does it carry conceptual pressure?
- does it sound translated?
- does it sound like public-sector prose?
- does it sound like generic AI-Swedish?
- does it preserve necessary roughness?
- does it allow Swedish compounds to work?
- does it overuse English?
- does it erase productive English-Swedish interference?
- does it sound too polished?

Classify:

- living Swedish register
- acceptable but needs author pass
- too generic
- too translated
- too polished
- too imitative
- not publishable

---

## 12. Publication status recommendation

Choose one:

- publishable as author-reviewed capsule
- publishable as AI-mediated capsule with disclosure
- usable internally
- requires manual author rewrite
- requires factual repair
- requires register repair
- hold as process artefact
- block publication

Explain.

---

## 13. Required repairs

List only necessary repairs.

Separate into:

### Must repair before publication

### Should repair manually

### Optional refinements

### Do not repair

Include “Do not repair” for roughness, pressure, odd phrasing, or instability that should remain.

---

## 14. Disclosure note

If publication is possible, write a short disclosure/status note.

Examples:

**Status:** AI-medierad registerrendering, granskad mot källmaterial och auktoritativ språkkroppsprofil.

**Status:** Processkapsel. Texten är AI-medierad och kräver inte status som författarprosa.

**Status:** Författargranskad kapsel baserad på källmaterial, inte ett litterärt originalstycke.

If no disclosure is needed, explain why.

---

## 15. Final verdict

Write one final paragraph.

It must answer:

- Does the text have contact?
- Does it preserve source status?
- Does it risk voice theft?
- Is it allowed to move toward publication?
- What is the one necessary next action?

Do not write more than 150 words.

---

# Failure conditions

Block publication if:

- AI-generated text claims authorial voice
- factual uncertainty has been erased
- source basis has been inflated
- the rendered text imitates the authority material too closely
- the rendered text is smoother but less true
- project terms have been replaced by generic frameworks
- the text sounds better than its contact permits
- the text cannot distinguish between source, rendering, diagnosis, and claim

---

# Core principle

A text does not pass audit because it sounds strong.

It passes audit because its strength has contact.
