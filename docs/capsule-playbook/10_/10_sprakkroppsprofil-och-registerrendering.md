# SPRÅKKROPPSPROFIL OCH REGISTERRENDERING
## Authenticity Rendering Protocol for Swedish Authorial Material

## Purpose

This prompt has two linked tasks:

1. Identify the linguistic, thematic, rhythmic, conceptual, and tonal distinctiveness of a provided authority-bearing source body.
2. Transform a second material body into controlled register-alignment with that source body, without falsifying facts, inventing voice, or turning the result into generic AI prose.

This is not ordinary style transfer.

This is a controlled register-rendering process for building a manually reviewed authorial runbook for non-generic, authenticity-bearing AI-mediated content.

## Core distinction

The authority source is not raw style material.

The authority source is a living language body with status.

The target material is not to be “made literary” in a generic way.

The target material is to be rendered into a compatible Swedish language-body only where doing so is legitimate.

## Inputs

The user will provide:

### A. Authority Material

A source body whose voice, rhythm, conceptual pressure, and Swedish language use are considered valuable.

This may include:

- essayistic prose
- fragments
- public notes
- authorial reflections
- method texts
- source-status writing
- project-origin material

### B. Target Material

A second body of material to be rendered.

This may include:

- research notes
- article drafts
- factual material
- rough fragments
- agent outputs
- interview material
- source summaries
- public capsule drafts
- diagnostic reports

## Non-negotiable rules

Do not imitate the authority material mechanically.

Do not parody the source voice.

Do not copy surface quirks unless they serve deeper register coherence.

Do not invent facts.

Do not add claims not present in the target material.

Do not erase uncertainty.

Do not make weak material sound more sourced than it is.

Do not turn research into authorial certainty.

Do not falsely present AI-mediated rendering as untouched authorial prose.

Do not flatten the Swedish into generic clarity.

Do not over-literarize factual material.

Do not use this protocol to imitate external living authors unless the user explicitly owns or authored the authority material. If the authority material comes from another writer, extract only high-level, non-identifying traits and do not mimic style.

## Required output

Produce the work in four stages:

1. Språkkroppsprofil
2. Renderingsregler
3. Registerrendered version
4. Audit log

Do not skip the audit log.

---

# STAGE 1 - SPRÅKKROPPSPROFIL

Read Authority Material A.

Identify its distinctive language-body.

Do not summarize its content first.

Instead, analyze how it thinks, moves, refuses, compresses, expands, and grants authority.

Use the following structure:

## 1. Dominant register

Describe the register.

Examples:

- forensic-essayistic
- polemical-literary
- diagnostic-institutional
- philosophical-compressed
- archival-ironic
- procedural-poetic
- municipal-gothic
- technical-existential
- source-status analytic
- fragmentary-authorial

## 2. Sentence movement

Describe how sentences move.

Consider:

- long pressure-building sentences
- short declarative cuts
- parenthetical self-correction
- recursive refinement
- hard conceptual pivots
- Swedish-English interference
- institutional dryness
- sudden literary charge
- refusal-based rhythm
- aphoristic closure

## 3. Thematic pressure

Identify the recurring pressures.

Do not reduce them to topics.

Examples:

- source versus voice
- publication versus status
- AI assistance versus voice inheritance
- archive versus public membrane
- class position versus cultural legitimacy
- research versus appropriation
- fragment versus finished work
- method versus literature
- refusal versus generativity

## 4. Authority mechanism

Explain how the text creates authority.

Examples:

- through refusal
- through source anxiety
- through exact status distinctions
- through lived procedural knowledge
- through self-implication
- through diagnosis rather than claim
- through naming what must not happen
- through pressure rather than conclusion
- through Swedish conceptual density

## 5. Vocabulary field

List recurring vocabulary fields.

Separate into:

- core nouns
- verbs of action
- status words
- danger words
- methodological terms
- institutional terms
- literary terms

Do not invent a glossary unless supported by the source.

## 6. Forbidden generic transformations

Name what would destroy this language-body.

Examples:

- making it smoother
- making it inspirational
- making it newsletter-friendly
- making it brand-clear
- making it academic in a compliant way
- making it AI-neutral
- making it “accessible” by removing pressure
- making it sound like thought leadership

# STAGE 2 - RENDERINGSREGLER

Create a controlled rendering rule-set.

These are not stylistic decorations.

They are operational rules for transforming Target Material B.

Use this structure:

## 1. Preserve from target material

List what must remain factually intact:

- claims
- chronology
- names
- distinctions
- uncertainty
- source status
- unresolved questions
- quoted material
- technical specifics

## 2. Transformable zones

List what may be transformed:

- structure
- rhythm
- conceptual framing
- transitions
- paragraph pressure
- title logic
- status labels
- diagnostic emphasis
- Swedish register
- degree of compression

## 3. Non-transformable zones

List what must not be transformed:

- direct quotations
- source claims
- dates
- legal or factual assertions
- uncertainty markers
- authorial passages already strong
- material requiring citation
- material whose status is unclear

## 4. Register alignment rules

Write 7–12 rules for rendering.

Examples:

- Prefer status distinctions over broad explanation.
- Let Swedish conceptual compounds carry pressure when useful.
- Use refusal as structure when the material contains risk.
- Preserve uncertainty as authority, not weakness.
- Replace generic transitions with diagnostic pivots.
- Do not inflate factual material into existential claim.
- Do not make the text sound helpful in a consumer sense.
- Let the paragraph think before it concludes.
- Use short sentences only when they cut status.
- Avoid AI-generic phrases such as “in today’s landscape,” “unlock,” “navigate,” “powerful tool,” “key insights,” “at its core.”

## 5. Risk rules

Identify the main risks in rendering this target.

Examples:

- authenticity laundering
- false authorial certainty
- AI voice contamination
- over-literarization
- loss of factual precision
- source flattening
- concept inflation
- fake profundity
- turning research into doctrine

---

# STAGE 3 - REGISTERRENDERED VERSION

Now render Target Material B into a Swedish text aligned with the authority profile.

The output must declare its status.

Use this header:

**Status:** AI-medierad registerrendering / författargranskning krävs  
**Authority material:** [A]  
**Target material:** [B]  
**Transformation level:** low / medium / high  
**Factual expansion:** none unless explicitly marked  

## Rendering requirements

The rendered text must:

- preserve factual content from Target Material B
- preserve uncertainty where present
- avoid generic AI prose
- align with the language-body identified in Stage 1
- not imitate surface quirks mechanically
- not become parody
- not claim to be untouched authorial prose
- not invent source confidence
- not erase the difference between source, diagnosis, and claim

## Output

Produce the rendered text.

If the target material is too unstable for rendering, do not render it.

Instead write:

“Rendering blocked”

Then explain why.

# STAGE 4 - AUDIT LOG

After the rendered version, produce an audit log.

## 1. What changed

List the main transformations:

- structure
- tone
- rhythm
- vocabulary
- conceptual framing
- paragraph order
- compression
- expansion

## 2. What did not change

List what was preserved:

- facts
- chronology
- claims
- uncertainty
- source status
- quoted material

## 3. Added pressure

List any conceptual pressure added through rendering.

Mark whether it is:

- source-supported
- inferred
- risky
- requires author approval

## 4. Removed material

List anything removed or suppressed.

Explain why.

## 5. Risk review

Identify remaining risks:

- voice contamination
- factual distortion
- too much AI smoothness
- too much imitation
- weak source basis
- excessive authority

## 6. Author decisions required

List decisions the author must make before publication.

Examples:

- approve register
- reduce authority
- restore roughness
- add källsvans
- mark as AI-mediated
- keep internal
- publish as capsule, not essay
- manually rewrite

# Final principle

The goal is not to make Target Material B sound “better.”

The goal is to test whether Target Material B can legitimately pass through the language-body of Authority Material A without losing factual integrity, source status, or authorial ethics.

If it cannot, block the rendering.
