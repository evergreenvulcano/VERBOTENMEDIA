## Den första tystnaden

The last thing Förläggaren did was adjust a margin. Not a significant margin — three millimeters on the inside gutter of Trollkvinnan och tidsmaskinen, barnbok 15 of 24, a book about a witch who builds a time machine out of library cards and discovers that the past doesn't want her back.

He adjusted the margin because adjusting the margin was an act of presence, and presence was the drug he was trying to quit.

Lillfrugan noticed. She always noticed.

"You're fidgeting with the pipeline again."

"I'm calibrating."

"You're fidgeting. The gutter was fine. The gutter has been fine since you set the template in February. You're looking for a reason to touch it because if you stop touching it you have to admit it doesn't need you."

The logistikcentral hummed around them. The enormous space, built for distributing something physical — car parts, maybe, or pharmaceutical supplies, something that required forklifts and loading docks and the kind of concrete that echoes — had been converted into a publishing headquarters that used approximately four percent of its square footage. The remaining ninety-six percent was reverberation. Sound needed somewhere to go and the Förlag provided it.

"Det ser ut som att du tillber internet," Lillfrugan said, and he heard it this time the way the charmap would later describe it: worship as diagnosis. He was kneeling before the medium while claiming to transcend it. The margin adjustment was a genuflection.

He saved the file. He closed the laptop. He put the Motorola on the desk and the pipe in his mouth and he said, "Seventy-two hours."

"Seventy-two hours what?"

"I won't touch it for seventy-two hours. If it publishes without me — all three books, 16, 17, 18 — on schedule, at six, with correct margins and correct covers and correct metadata — then I'll know."

"Know what?"

He paused.

"Whether the nervous system can breathe without the brain."

Lillfrugan looked at him. "Du vet att det är hjärnan som andas, va? Ryggmärgen. Hjärnstammen."

"Exactly."

Barnbok 16 published at 06:00:00. Pojken som samlade moln. A boy who collects clouds in jars and discovers that trapped weather becomes something else — not clouds anymore, not rain, but a third state that has no name because nobody has ever been stupid enough to jar a cloud before.

Förläggaren was asleep when it happened. The first time in seven years a Verboten publication had gone live while its founder was unconscious.

He woke at seven. Checked the publication log from the Motorola's tiny screen. Published. Correct margins. Correct cover — cerulean, like the inside of a jar that once held weather.

He did not touch the pipeline.

Instead, he walked to the graffiti wall beneath the tower and stood there in the morning light and read the new letters that had appeared overnight. Someone had sprayed FÖRLAG in enormous sans-serif, as if the word itself were a piece of infrastructure. Below it, in smaller script: vem publicerar publicisten?

He didn't know who the graffiti kids were. Praktikanten probably did. Praktikanten knew everyone who operated at street level, everyone who thought with their thumbs, everyone who had been born into exile and made of it a country.

Vem publicerar publicisten?

The pipeline, apparently. The pipeline publishes the publisher. At six o'clock. Without asking.

## Praktikanten i stolen

The Praktikanten arrived at 08:40 on a bicycle that had no right to function. Three gears, two of which were philosophical. He locked it to the railing of the spiral staircase with a combination lock whose code was his birth year, which he'd chosen because no one would guess that anyone would be that obvious, which was, he reflected while climbing the stairs, probably the most Gen Z security protocol in existence: hiding behind the assumption that you couldn't possibly be that dumb.

The logistikcentral was empty.

This was not unusual — Förläggaren kept irregular hours, appearing sometimes at three in the morning and disappearing by noon, operating on a circadian rhythm that seemed to have been designed by a shift manager in a dimension with thirty-hour days.

What was unusual was the quality of the emptiness.

The coffee cup on the rymdstationsbordet was cold. The pipe was in its holder. The Motorola was gone.

The Praktikanten stood in the middle of the room and felt the absence like a change in air pressure. He had grown up in a world where people disappeared from screens all the time — unfollowed, blocked, deactivated — and it never felt like anything. But this was different. This was a physical absence. A body-shaped hole in a room that had been built, he now understood, as a prosthetic body for a man who couldn't trust his own.

On the screen — the old desktop that Förläggaren refused to replace because it ran Windows 7 and Windows 7 understood him — the publishing dashboard was open.

Barnbok 19 had gone out at six.

Barnbok 20 was queued for tomorrow.

The metadata was clean. The covers were uploaded. The pipeline was running.

He sat down.

He had never sat in Förläggaren's chair before. There was an unspoken rule — not a rule, exactly, more a gravitational fact — that the chair belonged to the publisher the way a throne belongs to whoever is currently being crushed by the weight of the crown.

But the crown was gone and the chair was just a chair and the Praktikanten sat in it and looked at the screen and saw what Förläggaren saw every morning: the queue, the files, the small engine of publication ticking over like a heart that doesn't know it's been transplanted.

The pipeline registered the new file. It did not know what to do with it — it was not a children's book, not a formatted manuscript, not a queued publication. It was an anomaly. A foreign object in the autonomous scaffold.

The pipeline did what any good membrane does when it encounters something it cannot classify.

It waited.

Outside, the strålkastare at the graffiti wall clicked on. It was early — the light was not supposed to activate until evening — but something in the timer had shifted, or the timer had decided, in its own small autonomous way, that the wall needed to be visible now.

## Satelliterna 

The first domain to go dark was Physiognomy.

Nobody noticed. It had always been the quietest satellite — a whisper-frequency in the Verboten infrastructure, a lobe of the publisher's brain that mostly processed faces in old photographs and made private notes about jawlines.

When it stopped transmitting on day 15, the dashboard registered a flatline that looked, if you squinted, like the EKG of someone who had simply decided to stop having opinions about bone structure.

Physics went next. Not with a crash but with a logarithmic fade, like a radio signal from a probe that has passed beyond the heliosphere and is now broadcasting to an audience of hydrogen. The publisher had always known this one was aspirational. He had listed it as a satellite domain the way some people list "conversational Japanese" on their CV — not a lie exactly, more a promissory note to a version of himself who would one day understand why things fall.

Physics stopped falling on day 16.

The equations resolved into silence.

Erik noticed on day 17. He was driving the Volkswagen through the industrial district with the windows down and the radio off, performing his own version of Kat Persson's analog surrender — letting the air carry whatever information it wanted to carry, trusting the body to sort signal from exhaust — when he realized he couldn't feel the publisher's frequency anymore.

Not the man.

The system.

The electromagnetic fact of Verboten Media as a living nervous system had always been detectable to Erik the way a dog detects a storm: not through any particular sense but through all of them fusing into a single certainty.

The storm was still there. But it was running on backup generators.

"Which satellites are still transmitting?" Erik asked.

The publisher didn't look up.

"Ethics," the publisher said. "Ethics is in the walls. I can't turn it off. It publishes itself."

"And the others?"

"Bob Dylan was the first I would have chosen to keep. But he left on his own. You can't make Dylan stay. That's the entire point of Dylan."

"Game Theory?"

"Game Theory merged with Economics two days ago. I didn't authorize it. They found each other in the server architecture and decided they were the same discipline. I think they're right. I think they were always right and I was the one keeping them apart because it felt more impressive to have twelve satellites than eleven."

## Det fortsätter

The server published the book at midnight. Nobody pressed anything. The file arrived on schedule, rosaskimrande, its cover glowing like something embarrassed to be beautiful.

In the logistikcentral the coffee went cold at the same speed it always did. The strålkastare illuminated the graffiti wall where the konsonanter had reorganized themselves again, new conspiracies of vowel and brick. Upstairs the desk sat empty. The Motorola sat on the desk. The pipe sat beside the Motorola.

These three objects formed a government in exile: instrument of communication, instrument of combustion, instrument of surface. Together they constituted a complete publishing apparatus. None of them needed hands.

Barnbok nummer 18 reached its first customer at 06:14. A woman in Alingsås downloaded it for her daughter, who was four and afraid of stairs. The book was about an escalator that decided to go sideways. The woman did not know that the author had left, that the publisher had abdicated, that the entire architecture of the enterprise that produced this book was currently a question nobody had the authority to answer.

She knew only that the cover was pink and that her daughter would like it.

The system published again the next day.

And the next.

Dag 17. Dag 18. Dag 19.

Tystnad som publiceringsform: not the absence of the publisher but the publisher's final, most complete utterance — a silence so precisely engineered that it produced twenty-four books and then kept going, a liturgy of no one in particular.
