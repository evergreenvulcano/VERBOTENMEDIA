## draft-run-1-2026-04-22

# The Signal at Nineteen Fourteen

---

Marta Sundqvist had been covering Jens Spräck for eleven years without ever meeting him in a room where he didn't already own the air.

She'd watched him stand on a loading dock in Härnösand and declare the region's fishing surplus a missed musical. She'd watched him press his palm flat against a server rack as if taking its pulse. She'd listened to him describe a competing telecommunications firm as "the adult diaper of ambition" — live, on public radio, without correction, without apology, and somehow without consequence.

She had not, in eleven years, been surprised by him.

That ended on a November Sunday.

She was at her desk at quarter past seven in the evening when her scanner — a battered Uniden she'd inherited from a crime correspondent in Umeå — locked onto something at 92.4 FM that was not supposed to be there. The frequency was dead. Had been for years. She could check the license registry. She had checked it, once, following a tip about unlicensed transmitters in the Bothnian hinterland. The range was silent. Municipal maintenance boredom.

What she heard instead was Jens Spräck, clearly drunk, not performing.

He was speaking in a register she didn't recognize. Slower. The declarative bluntness was still there, but without the crowd behind it, the syntax opened like a coat that had finally been unbuttoned. He talked about a machine. He called it the Tenderness Engine. He described it the way a man might describe a house he had demolished — in measurements that were now only memory.

*It doesn't make anything*, he said. *That's the point. Everything we build makes something. Even the failures make wreckage, and wreckage makes a story, and a story makes a pitch, and a pitch makes a project, and a project makes a tombstone shaped like a milestone. This thing — if it works — makes nothing. It just lets something already there get heard.*

She sat very still for forty minutes. The interview, or monologue, or confession — she couldn't tell who was conducting it, whether anyone was — ran until 19:54 and then the frequency returned to its official silence.

She went to bed certain she'd imagined it.

---

By the third Sunday she had a recording and a question.

By the fifth she had three sources: a parking warden in Skellefteå named Bertil who had, the week after a Sunday broadcast, forgiven seventeen violations in a single morning and could not explain why; a pair of brothers in Piteå who had been litigating their father's will for six years and who, in the third week of November, met in a carpark and agreed to split everything without lawyers — then separately called Marta to describe the meeting as *strange, like something had shifted in the weather*; and an infrastructure procurement officer for a mid-sized municipality whose name she had agreed not to use, who had wept in the stairwell between floors four and five, on a Tuesday, for reasons that felt to her like the stairwell itself had earned something.

None of them had listened to 92.4 FM. None of them had heard of the Tenderness Engine.

---

The signal, when she finally triangulated it, came from somewhere between a transmission substation and a wetland conservation zone eighteen kilometers northwest of a town she'd only visited once, for a courthouse story, in 2019.

She drove out on the fifth Sunday of her investigation, arriving at 18:50. She parked on a gravel road. She carried the Uniden and a thermos. She walked northeast along a firebreak until the signal peaked.

The source was a caravan.

It was a very old caravan, the kind whose caramel-colored aluminum panels suggest the 1970s without nostalgia. It sat in a clearing that had been cleared by something other than intention — a tree had come down long enough ago that the stump had gone soft, and someone had placed a trestle table on top of it and covered the table with radio equipment: a transmitter, a reel-to-reel, several car batteries wired in series, and a single strip of LED lighting that gave the whole arrangement the quality of a dentist's office operating during a power outage.

In the folding chair behind the table sat a man she recognized.

He did not look up immediately. He was adjusting something on the reel-to-reel with the focused attention of someone who had stopped performing focus entirely. His hands were cold, she could tell from the way he moved them. He was wearing a down vest over a thermal and he had not shaved in what looked like five days. He was heavier than his press photos, which was not unusual; what was unusual was that the heaviness looked unperformed.

*You're not from the utility company*, he said, still without looking up.

*No.*

*Good. They've been trying to find this since March.* He turned a knob. *Sit down.*

There was no second chair. She stood.

*I heard the broadcasts*, she said.

*I assumed someone would eventually.* He pressed play. The reel began to move. *Are you here to write about it?*

*I don't know yet*, she said, which was true.

He looked at her then for the first time. He had the eyes of a man who had just watched someone try to purchase something that wasn't for sale and found the attempt more interesting than annoying.

*It's not a project*, he said. *I want you to understand that. I'm not launching anything. There's no platform. There's no token. There's no board.* He paused. *There's barely a reel-to-reel.*

*What is it?*

He looked back at the equipment. *Eleven years ago I said something to a sound engineer in Gothenburg that I've been trying to undo ever since. I told him that feeling was a format problem. That people didn't feel things deeply because the containers were wrong.* A short silence. *I believed it. I still believe it, partially. But I built the next twelve years around the container theory and left the feeling unexamined.*

The reel turned. The LED strip buzzed slightly.

*The machine doesn't exist*, he said. *I've told myself that. But there's an interview — I don't remember making it, I was in a bad state that winter — where I described it as if it did. As if I'd built it somewhere and left it running. And I've been playing that interview on Sundays because — * He stopped. Looked at the wetland, which was dark and invisible beyond the clearing. *I don't know. Because maybe if I say the thing enough times, reality will get embarrassed and produce it.*

*People are being affected*, Marta said. *By the broadcasts. Even people who aren't listening.*

He was quiet long enough that she thought he hadn't heard her.

Then he said: *That's the part I don't talk about.*

---

She asked him what the machine would do, if it existed.

He thought about it for a while. Not the way he thought in press conferences, where thinking was a gesture made between the setup and the punchline. He thought the way people think when no one is expected to be watching.

*It doesn't amplify*, he said finally. *Every machine I've ever backed amplifies. Makes something bigger, faster, louder, more available. Even the failures were at least enthusiastic about scale.* A small dry sound that wasn't quite a laugh. *This would go the other direction. It would — reduce the overhead. The social overhead. On things people are already feeling.* He rubbed the back of his neck. *You know when someone is sorry and they can't say it because the cost of saying it has gotten too high? The social math around the apology has inflated past the original wound? This would deflate the math. Not fake the feeling. Just — clear some of the debt off the top so the actual feeling can pass.*

*That sounds*, Marta said carefully, *like something that would destroy you publicly.*

He looked at her. *Yes.*

*If it worked.*

*If it worked,* he confirmed. *You can't monetize it. You can't brand it. You can't take credit for what it does because what it does is make people briefly more themselves. And you can't even claim the invention because the machine doesn't exist and probably can't.* A pause. *The interview where I describe it — I was never that coherent again about it. I tried to write it down later. It came out as business strategy. Everything I try to write down comes out as business strategy. That's the trap.* He touched the reel-to-reel. *So instead I just play the tape.*

The broadcast window was approaching. He began his preparations with the solemnity of a man who had built a ritual around something he refused to call a ritual.

*Are you going to publish this?* he asked.

*I don't know*, she said again.

*If you do —* He aligned the reel. *Don't call it a project. Don't call it a venture. Don't give it a launch date or a funding round or a region or a pilot. Don't let anyone ask me about the roadmap.* He looked at the LED strip. *If you write it, write it as something that might not be real. Write it as a signal someone heard once at the wrong time on an unlicensed frequency and couldn't explain.*

*That's what it is*, she said.

*Exactly*, he said.

He pressed play.

At 19:14, Jens Spräck's voice moved out across the frozen marsh, and whatever it did to the dark, no one would be able to measure.

Marta stood in the clearing and listened, and wrote nothing down.
