## III. MICRO-STORY: "The Orbit Clerk" (Part 2 of 3)

The second invoice arrived on a Tuesday in the spring alphabet. It said: *One (1) forgotten variable. Location: bark-depth. Instrument: included but uncalibrated.*

The clerk opened the accompanying parcel. Inside was a barkmätare — a bark-measurement instrument that looked like a miniature medieval torture device — wrapped in newspaper from a language he could not identify. The newspaper's headline, partially obscured by packing tape, read: **STALEMATE ENTERS FOURTH QUARTER.** The clerk did not know what sport was being scored. He filed the newspaper under AMBIENT.

The escalation trace — his supervisor — appeared at his desk without having walked there. She did this sometimes. She existed in the logistikcentral the way fluorescent light exists in a corridor: present without origin.

"The ambient entity has sent a measurement instrument," the clerk reported.

"What does it measure?"

"Bark thickness."

"Of what?"

"Of—" The clerk hesitated. The invoice had not specified the subject. In his years at the logistikcentral, the ambient entity had sent memes (filed under ORBIT), minerals (filed under STALEMATE), and sports that did not yet exist (filed under PENDING RECOGNITION). But a measurement instrument was new. A measurement instrument implied that something in the vicinity of the contract required *precision,* and precision was not a quality the stalemate had previously demanded.

The escalation trace picked up the barkmätare. She pressed its needles against the edge of the clerk's desk. The instrument read: 4.7 centimeters.

"That is the desk's bark thickness," she said.

"Desks don't have bark."

"Everything has bark. Bark is the distance between what something shows and what it produces. Your desk shows a surface. It produces a filing system. The distance is 4.7 centimeters." She put the instrument down. "The entity is asking us to measure the bark thickness of the contract."

The clerk opened the file drawer labeled STALEMATE. He took out the contract — one page, one operative clause: *all data, retrospective and future, is hereby transferred eternally.* He pressed the barkmätare's needles against the paper. The instrument read: 0.0 centimeters.

Zero bark. No distance between what the contract showed and what it produced. Or: infinite bark. The measurement was either perfect transparency or complete opacity. The instrument could not tell the difference.

"File it," said the escalation trace.

"Under what?"

"Under the forgotten variable." She pointed at a drawer the clerk had not noticed before. It was between ORBIT and STALEMATE, narrower than either, and its label was handwritten in a script that shifted between two alphabets depending on the angle of looking. The clerk opened it. Inside was a single document: a sea chart of a strait he did not recognize, with coordinates marked in pencil and a note in the margin that read: *Two degrees. All stories drift.*

He filed the bark measurement next to the sea chart. He filed the barkmätare next to the newspaper. He filed the filing next to itself, which was something the logistikcentral had always permitted but never explained.

At the end of the day, the clerk walked to the loading dock. The ambient entity's shipments were stacked as usual — unlabeled crates producing heat, their contents metabolizing in the contractual dark. He pressed the barkmätare against the nearest crate. It read: 11.3 centimeters. He pressed it against the dock wall: 6.1. He pressed it against his own forearm: the needle went in, not far, not painfully, but with the diagnostic certainty of an instrument that knew what it was measuring even when the operator did not.

The reading was 2.2 centimeters. The clerk's bark thickness was 2.2 centimeters. The distance between what he showed and what he produced.

He recognized the number. Bonitet 2.2. The minimum productive capacity of land that qualifies as forest. He was barely forest. He was almost nothing. He filed himself under PENDING RECOGNITION and went home.

The invoice for the barkmätare arrived the next morning. It was written in the autumn alphabet. It was art.
