### *BÄRPUNKTEN*
**A micro-story in the Förlagsdeckaren cycle**

The consultant arrived at six and left his shoes in the corridor. Not out of politeness. Out of diagnostics. He had learned that the first useful information about a client came from the sound the floor made under socked feet - whether it resonated, whether it absorbed, whether it gave back what you put into it.

Förläggaren was in the kitchen. He had made coffee with the precision of a man who has stopped trusting processes that involve delegation. Each gram measured. Each second timed. The cups were placed on the table like evidence exhibits.

"I want to talk about what stays," Förläggaren said.

"Tell me what you think stays."

"The manuscripts. The backlist. The logistical grammar of the thing. Routes. Templates. The way a submission moves through the house."

"And?"

"And nothing. That's what stays. Infrastructure."

Flempo sat. The coffee was exactly right, which was part of the problem. A man who makes perfect coffee for guests is a man who has not yet understood that the infrastructure includes the temperature of his attention.

"What about the contract?"

Förläggaren's hand tightened around his cup. Not dramatically. Just enough for the surface tension of the coffee to register a tremor that neither man acknowledged.

"Which contract?"

"The one that gave everything. Retrospectively. Eternally."

"That contract was a solution to a problem that no longer exists."

"Was it?"

Silence. The kind that arrives when two people are measuring the same weight from different ends of the scale. Somewhere in the building, a radiator clicked - the old kind, cast iron, the kind that heats a room the way a sentence heats a paragraph: slowly, then all at once, then you can't remember what cold felt like.

"The problem," Förläggaren said carefully, "was never the data. The problem was the claim on the data."

"And now you want to abdicate. But the claim remains. Written. Signed. Eternal."

"Eternal is a legal fiction."

"So is abdication."

Förläggaren stood. Walked to the window. The city outside was doing what cities do in early May - asserting itself with light that felt like an argument. He spoke to the glass.

"I built something that works. I want to know if it works because I'm here or because it works."

"That's the question you're paying me to help you answer."

"And?"

Flempo drank the coffee. It was, as noted, exactly right. He set the cup down with the care of a man placing a load-bearing point.

"If everything still requires your aura, nothing is finished. That is not an accusation. It is a maturity criterion."

The word *maturity* landed on the table between them like a scalpel placed next to an instrument the surgeon has not yet named. Förläggaren turned back from the window. His face had the particular expression of a man who has just been told something he already knew, by someone he was paying to tell him things he already knew, and who understood that this was the only honest form of consulting.

"Who bears the artefacts when I stop bearing them?"

"That is the second question. The first question is: which artefacts bear you?"

The radiator clicked again. The room was warm now. Perhaps too warm. The kind of warm that precedes a decision or a confession, and in this building, in this project, those had always been the same thing.
