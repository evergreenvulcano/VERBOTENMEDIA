## IV. OUTPUT FORMAT 1 - MICRO-STORY

### *The Consultant's Sauna*

**Part 1 of 3: The Question Before the Question**

Flempo arrived forty minutes early and spent the time sitting in the parking lot with the engine off, rereading the dossier on his phone. He had written it himself three days ago, but it already felt like someone else's work. The phrases were too clean. *Bearer-identification.* *Trust-surface engineering.* These were the words of a man who had not yet entered the room with the client.

The building was a converted textile warehouse on the edge of Borås. Someone had installed a wood-fired sauna in what used to be the dyeing room. The walls still held a faint chemical sweetness - something between lavender and industrial solvent. The squash court was upstairs, unheated, with windows that leaked November.

Förläggaren was already inside, sitting on the lower bench in the changing room, wearing the expression of a man who had paid significant money to be uncomfortable.

"Du kom," he said.

"Jag kom."

They did not shake hands. They had passed that formality years ago, and what had replaced it was something worse: a mutual recognition that neither of them was entirely sure what register to use. Friend. Consultant. Opponent. Co-conspirator in a project that kept changing names.

The dossier had listed six buttons to press. Flempo had already decided to press none of them. The dossier was preparation, not script. The real work was listening for the sentence Förläggaren would say that contained the actual question, which would not be the question he had paid to ask.

They entered the sauna. Eighty-two degrees. The stones had been heating since morning. Förläggaren poured water without asking. The steam rose and the room contracted.

"I want to know," Förläggaren said, eyes on the stones, "whether anything I've built can stand without me."

Flempo waited. In his experience, the first version of any important question was a decoy.

"Because I've been thinking about this for eight months now, and every time I try to answer it honestly, I end up back in the same place. Which is: I don't know. I genuinely don't know. And the not-knowing is starting to - not *hurt*, exactly. But it's becoming structural."

Flempo nodded. *Structural* was the word. Not emotional, not philosophical - structural. The publisher was describing a load-bearing failure, and he was doing it in the only language he trusted, which was architectural.

"What do you mean by *stand*?" Flempo asked.

Förläggaren looked at him. The steam made his face harder to read than it should have been.

"I mean: will anyone continue to do the work the way it should be done, once I'm no longer the one deciding what *should* means?"

There it was. Not *can the institution survive* but *can my standards survive*. Two entirely different questions. The first was logistical. The second was existential, and it was the one that no dossier could answer, because the answer depended on something Förläggaren was not yet ready to consider: that his standards might already have left his body. That they might already live in documents, in folder structures, in the way certain people had learned to argue without being told how to argue. That the publisher's fear of departure was based on a premise that might no longer be true - that he was still the sole carrier of his own intentions.

The stalemate between Spräck and Verboten sat somewhere in the corners of the room like heat that had nowhere to go. Two contracts. Two entities that could not resolve their friction without dissolving the friction's value. Förläggaren's abdication question was the same question wearing different clothes: *What happens to the tension when one pole removes itself?*

Flempo closed his eyes. "I'm not going to answer that question today," he said. "What I'm going to do is ask you to name three things that must not be vulgarized after you leave. And then we'll see whether those things live inside you or have already found other addresses."

The steam hissed. Förläggaren said nothing for thirty-eight seconds. Flempo counted.

"The knife," Förläggaren said finally. "The dead child. And the volcano."

"Those are novel elements," Flempo said. "I asked about the institution."

"I know what you asked."

The silence after that was the first useful silence of the session. Flempo let it work. In the dossier he had written: *Body before doctrine. Squash is not decoration.* But now he understood that the body in question was not Förläggaren's. It was the body of the work. And the work had a pulse that neither of them could measure from inside this room.

*End of Part 1.*
