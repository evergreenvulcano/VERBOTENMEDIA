i. Day 15 — The Last Intervention

The logistikcentral hummed around them. The enormous space, built for distributing something physical — car parts, maybe, or pharmaceutical supplies, something that required forklifts and loading docks and the kind of concrete that echoes — had been converted into a publishing headquarters that used approximately four percent of its square footage. The remaining ninety-six percent was reverberation. Sound needed somewhere to go and the Förlag provided it.

"Det ser ut som att du tillber internet," Lillfrugan said, and he heard it this time the way the charmap would later describe it: worship as diagnosis. He was kneeling before the medium while claiming to transcend it. The margin adjustment was a genuflection.

"Seventy-two hours what?"

"I won't touch it for seventy-two hours. If it publishes without me — all three books, 16, 17, 18 — on schedule, at six, with correct margins and correct covers and correct metadata — then I'll know."

"Know what?"

He paused. Outside, through the vast industrial windows, Gothenburg's dusk was doing the thing it does in April — not quite arriving, not quite leaving, hanging in the frequency between light and its absence like a sentence that refuses to end.

"Whether the nervous system can breathe without the brain."

Barnbok 16 published at 06:00:00. Pojken som samlade moln. A boy who collects clouds in jars and discovers that trapped weather becomes something else — not clouds anymore, not rain, but a third state that has no name because nobody has ever been stupid enough to jar a cloud before.

He woke at seven. Checked the publication log from the Motorola's tiny screen. Published. Correct margins. Correct cover — cerulean, like the inside of a jar that once held weather.

He did not touch the pipeline.

Instead, he walked to the graffiti wall beneath the tower and stood there in the morning light and read the new letters that had appeared overnight. Someone had sprayed FÖRLAG in enormous sans-serif, as if the word itself were a piece of infrastructure. Below it, in smaller script: vem publicerar publicisten?

He didn't know who the graffiti kids were. Praktikanten probably did. Praktikanten knew everyone who operated at street level, everyone who thought with their thumbs, everyone who had been born into exile and made of it a country. The generational transmission was happening down here, on the wall, in spray paint, in a question that the novel hadn't yet learned to answer.

Vem publicerar publicisten?

The pipeline, apparently. The pipeline publishes the publisher. At six o'clock. Without asking.

iii. Day 17 — The Interview

Erik arrived at quarter to midnight. At midnight, barnbok 18 published.

Erik looked at Förläggaren. Förläggaren looked at Erik. Between them: the silence that is not absence but publication. The silence that proves the nervous system breathes without the brain. "Tre dagar," Förläggaren said.

"Tre dagar," Erik agreed.

And in Trieste, a white cat on a windowsill stretched, and Kat Persson turned a page of the Gazzetta and understood none of it and this was, he thought, the most honest form of reading — to encounter language without the violence of comprehension, to let the shapes be shapes, to let the meaning arrive late, like a message from a network that sings only after its signals cease to matter.

The pipeline hummed. Barnbok 19 entered the queue.

It would publish at six. It would publish at six regardless.
