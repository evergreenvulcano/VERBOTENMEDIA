# Method: Thought-Figure as Ontological Design of the Total Project

## Thought-figure

Radically subjective fiction with generative output: specific enough to take risks, portable enough to be used by others.

See: the thought-figure (there can be as many as needed), straight and long from a bird’s-eye perspective, gaze steady inside its closed tent. The square tent cloth is its fragile shell, but also its adventurous character. This tent, this square, is also inside another square—inside another square—membrane on membrane on membrane: they relate to one another, yet are sufficient in themselves. On the corners of the squares sketched on the white sheet, write the words that constitute the world of the thought-figure. See: the squares are like wavelengths—among other things mental—and they pulse out from the thought-figure’s frontal bone like a comic-book villain.

The square-stream ends somewhere only to return, as if the thought-figure were a portal or a transitional phase. The thought-figure is so unavoidably resonant with the World that… it is a work.

A model becomes substrate for a further generative model. The wheel of feelings can become a reference bank of modules that can in turn be patched in for signal synthesis.

Parody should be made with love, but much else can be made out of love. So the parody-wheel consists of a character gallery with iconic tropes, which in turn can have metadata with further references.

Countermove against martyr-complex: the difference between being a victim and making a sacrifice.

The author-role as a “social” function: a channel into the collective consciousness for anyone who wants to plant things there.

Another aspect of the story: hyperindividualism embodied in a concrete art project during a coming-of-age period.

They said individualism was spreading and theorized about how it was harmful and why it should be resisted, more than 50 years ago. Here we are now, and insofar as art resonates with others we have proof that it really exists in us in a wholly new way than before; deeper and deeper culture drills into psyches.

To always be there, on sufferance. On the other hand—how sick, how sick: believe it or not, the one who rejects weight in favor of sloth runs the risk of “being ironic”!

More on the story: an initial stimulant-trigger paired with a catalytic encounter with a philosopher-text in the early-2010s—an utterly wild practice detonated. In the artifacts, a clear before-and-after appears: the poetic subject starts running with its vision instead of quietly observing it and mirroring unclear feeling-streams against its being. Once that leap is taken, it no longer mirrors; it becomes certain it can, through language, deconstruct every ridiculous proof that life is worth living.

And somewhere in refusal the poetic subject is right: if a system produces that verdict, then the only viable move is to incorporate the verdict’s structure as material—otherwise the apparatus lies. Somewhere in the follow-through, the clipped omnipotent stance breaks into song: fictional extreme-song of the kind idols perform. The poetic subject detects quality as a signal state; if it has occurred once under these constraints, it can recur under renewed conditions.

After that, the texts run at full speed, and each time the momentum fades due to reality-friction or input scarcity, a new philosopher-text steps in; a few pages from any of several traditions and the fireworks begin again, and the poetic-subject spirit is re-amplified in its conviction that there is value in language-generation. The poetic subject experiences rolling with titans and “having something” worth preserving. But, as with everything not-yet-embodied, resistance appears: the body-function turns into an opposing constraint. External interventions and self-modulation enter the loop (chemistry, meditation protocols, embodied psychology as technique).

And after a collapse at the edge of the apparatus’s overload—at a threshold where escalation would not be an absurd leap but a destructive one—the poetic subject withdraws while the body-function takes precedence: a process as slow as the idealist art-process. Time-marker: mid-2010s.

And, as a preservation strategy, the poetic subject is set aside—slowly, then finally (late-2010s), deliberately forgotten. Governance shifts: lower-level maintenance loops override narrative-production; higher-level intention rides along and misfires on trivialities until the pendulum turns back again. To see certain actions as preservation strategy is a grounding—another narratological spell that counteracts competing perspectives: those forced by the maintenance layer’s unavoidable inputs, and those the poetic subject admits sparingly to rebalance its position in abstract rhizome-space.

This journey can be rewritten from any number of angles. The hero’s journey is a cultural trope that does not serve the work, and it is not what the context needs: an individual ascent—toward what, exactly? Even if entropy is locally reduced, entropy has operated; nothing is redeemed by that fact alone. The ground of the final product must be vast in quantity and precision, not satisfied with the pleasant sensation of revenge or self-congratulation. The whole involves distributed cost—borne by many more than any single figure.

## Hermeneutics

In technical fields (especially systems engineering, computing, and design), an interface is the boundary or surface of interaction between two distinct systems, components, or actors. Your framing of it as the “surface of a black box that a human user can see” fits well with one common case (user interfaces), but technically the definition is broader.

Core technical definition

An interface is a specified boundary through which information, control signals, or actions pass between systems without requiring knowledge of the internal implementation of either side.

In black-box terms:

A system is treated as a black box (its internal workings are hidden).

The interface is the exposed surface where inputs can be provided and outputs can be observed.

The interface defines what interactions are allowed and how they occur, but not how the system internally produces results.

Important nuance about visibility

Your statement emphasizes the human-visible surface, which is technically a specific subtype:

Human-facing interface: the part of a system visible and usable by humans.
Example: graphical controls, command prompts, dashboards.

System interface: interactions between machines or software modules that humans may not see.
Example: APIs, communication protocols, electrical connectors.

So technically:

An interface is the formalized interaction boundary of a system, representing the externally accessible part of a black box—visible to humans, other software, or hardware—through which inputs are accepted and outputs are delivered.

Formal black-box interpretation

In strict systems terminology:

Black box: internal structure hidden.

Interface: the contract at the boundary describing:

Inputs (allowed signals, commands, data)

Outputs (responses, data, state changes)

Interaction rules (formats, timing, protocols)

In your phrasing (refined technical version)

If we rewrite your statement more technically:

An interface is the externally exposed boundary of a black-box system that defines the interactions accessible to an observer or user, representing the portion of the system that can be perceived, addressed, or manipulated without knowledge of its internal structure.

Great — let’s continue across the main technical domains where the term “interface” has a precise meaning, while keeping your black-box surface idea as the conceptual anchor. In each field, the interface is the part of the system that is intentionally exposed so interaction can occur without revealing the internal mechanism.

Interfaces Across Technical Domains

1. Systems Theory & Cybernetics

This is the domain closest to the concept you described.

In systems theory, a system is modeled as a black box with:

inputs

outputs

internal state (hidden)

a boundary

The interface is the operational boundary where the environment interacts with the system.

Technically:

It defines the channels of exchange (energy, information, material).

It determines what the environment is allowed to influence.

So in this field, your idea can be expressed as:

The interface is the observable and controllable boundary surface of a system through which the environment interacts with an otherwise opaque mechanism.

Examples:

Control panel of a reactor

Sensor inputs to an automated system

Data feed into a model

Here, visibility is optional — the interface may be conceptual rather than visual.

2. Software Engineering (Programming Interfaces)

In software, the interface is the callable surface of a program.

This is usually called an API (Application Programming Interface).

The interface defines:

available functions

inputs (parameters)

outputs (return values)

error behaviors

protocols

Important distinction:
The interface is not the code that performs the work, only the contract for interaction.

Black-box view:

Program Internals

(hidden algorithms)

API Interface

(functions, endpoints)

External Programs

Example:
A database system exposes an interface like:

query(database, statement)

insert(record)

delete(id)

The internal indexing, caching, or storage mechanisms remain hidden.

Key property in software:
Interface stability allows implementation changes without breaking users.

3. Object-Oriented Programming

Here the word interface has an additional formal meaning.

An interface is a type that specifies methods without implementing them.

It defines:

capabilities a class must provide

method signatures

behavioral expectations

Example conceptually:

Interface: Drawable

    draw()

    resize()

Class: Circle implements Drawable

The interface here is a pure behavioral surface of an object.

This is very close to the abstract boundary of the black box.

4. User Interface (UI / HCI)

This is the specific case you initially described.

A user interface is the human-perceivable surface of the system.

It includes:

visual elements

interaction controls

feedback mechanisms

Examples:

Graphical UI (buttons, windows)

Command-line interface

Voice interface

Gesture interface

Here the interface is literally the visible side of the black box.

Internally:

System logic

Data processing

Algorithms

Externally:

User Interface

This is often called the presentation layer.

Important technical principle:
UI is only one interface of a system.

Most systems have many.

5. Hardware Engineering

In hardware, an interface is a physical and electrical boundary.

It defines:

connector type

signal format

voltage levels

communication protocol

timing

Examples:

USB interface

HDMI interface

PCIe interface

network ports

Here the interface is literally the contact surface between machines.

Black-box representation:

Device Internals

(chips, logic)

Hardware Interface

(port, pins, signals)

Other Device

In this domain, the interface specification is extremely strict.

6. Network Engineering

In networking, an interface is a communication endpoint.

Example:
A network interface includes:

IP address

protocol stack

data transmission rules

Typical meaning:

network interface card (NIC)

logical network interface

API endpoint on a server

Here the interface defines:

how messages enter the system

how responses leave

7. Operating Systems

Operating systems define several layers of interfaces:

Kernel Interface

Interface between:

hardware

system core

System Call Interface

Interface between:

applications

operating system

Example:

read()

write()

open()

fork()

Programs interact with the OS only through this interface.

Again:
The OS internals remain hidden.

8. Control Systems Engineering

Here the interface is the control surface of a machine.

Examples:

control inputs

sensor outputs

actuator signals

Example system:

Plant (machine)

↓

Controller Interface

↓

Operator or Automation

The interface determines:

what can be controlled

what can be measured

Unifying Technical Definition

Across all technical domains, the word interface consistently means:

The defined boundary of a system that exposes permitted interactions while concealing internal implementation.

Or more formally:

An interface is the externally accessible interaction surface of a system, specified by rules governing inputs, outputs, and allowable operations, independent of the system’s internal structure.

Important Insight (Often Missed)

The interface is not just a surface — it is also a contract.

Technically an interface contains three things:

Exposure
What the outside world can access.

Interaction Rules
How interaction must occur.

Abstraction Boundary
What remains hidden.

This is why interfaces are fundamental to:

modular systems

engineering scalability

software architecture

complex machine design

One More Deeper Systems View (Useful Concept)

A complex system often has multiple interfaces layered:

Human Interface

Software Interface

System Interface

Hardware Interface

Physical Interface

Each layer hides the one beneath it.

This is known as interface abstraction layering.

It is a core principle of modern engineering.
