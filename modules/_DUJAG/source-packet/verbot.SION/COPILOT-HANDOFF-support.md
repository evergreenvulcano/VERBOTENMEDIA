As a systems document, the handoff is strong in directional pressure and weak in admission logic.

Its main strength is that it clearly names the intended semantic posture. It distinguishes constitutive from runtime, positions file from character instruction, underinstance from plugin, expressive from diagnostic, and archive from active layer. That is real structure, not just tone. The required scaffold, design rules, and forbidden drift section are unusually crisp, and they do meaningful work rather than decorative work  . It also correctly refuses deletion of unstable material and insists on provenance and retention, which is one of the few reliable anti-amnesia moves in the document .

Where it is semantically underbound:

The largest gap is source admission. The handoff says “use the packet around `Dujag.md` as the conceptual basis” and assigns sharp roles to `runtime.md`, `morning-estimation-anchor.md`, `midday-compression.md`, and `merge-dossier-continuity-memory.md`, but it never establishes a dependency gate for what happens if those files are incomplete, contradictory, absent, or weaker than assumed . It behaves as if source status were already settled.

That matters because the handoff is trying to create something close to a constitutive layer. The repo-grounded drafting law you’ve already established elsewhere is stricter: determine what exists, what is implied, what is missing, and stop when the source is insufficient rather than hallucinating canon into existence  . The handoff does not carry that discipline forward. It presumes a module before it proves one.

It is also underbound on semantic admissibility. It says what files should exist and what they should generally do, but not what would count as a failed constitutive reading. It asks for “protected semantic intensity” and “semantic sovereignty,” but those remain mostly declarative rather than operational unless the attached packet already provides stable tests for them . In practice, that means Copilot can satisfy the folder contract while still misunderstanding the actual semantic core.

A second underbinding is module identity. `/modules/<new-module>/` is structurally convenient, but ontologically empty. There is no naming rule, no criterion for when this is a new module rather than a proposal surface, no threshold for when the packet deserves module status at all. That creates a risk of premature canonization by folder creation alone.

A third underbinding is authority. The document says the new module must remain separate from `/_gpt`, but it never states what tier this new module belongs to. Is it experimental canon, proposal canon, source-mass, or support runtime? Without that, the scaffold can quietly become a truth engine simply because it is better organized than the source batch.

Where it is overbound:

It hardcodes a fairly elaborate file scaffold before proving the material needs every one of those surfaces. `README.md`, `source-intake.md`, `constitutive-spec.md`, `runtime-composition.md`, `underinstance-contracts.md`, `glossary.md`, `diagnostic-review.md`, and `archive/initiation-memoria.md` may be right, but the handoff treats them as mandatory architecture rather than as the result of gap detection . That is overbinding by template.

The language policy is also slightly overbound. English-core architecture with Swedish-aware protected terms is a sound default, but in this formulation it risks forcing bilingual formalism too early, before it is clear which Swedish terms are actually load-bearing and which are merely local phrasing . A system can become very correct about its bilingual control entries while already having flattened the thing it was trying to preserve.

It is also overbound in its acceptance checklist. The checklist optimizes for surface completeness: file presence, archive path, normalization, terminology notes, documented distinctions, stated drifts guarded against . Those are good as hygiene, but they are poor as proof of retention. This is exactly the zone where a system collapses into formal correctness.

Where it is likely to collapse into formal correctness without real retention:

The primary failure mode is checklist compliance replacing semantic memory. A Copilot run can satisfy every visible acceptance criterion and still silently weaken `Dujag.md` into style, weaken empathy into a glossary note, weaken third-eye/locus into a camera disclaimer, and weaken unstable fragments into curated archival debris. The document is very strong at naming what must not happen, but weaker at specifying how one would detect that it has happened anyway  .

A second collapse risk is normalization. The handoff says normalize all incoming materials into markdown, preserve provenance, preserve contradictions, preserve unstable fragments, do not destroy rejected material . That is good. But normalization itself is one of the highest-risk semantic compression steps. If the operator lacks a strict “what cannot be rewritten” rule, the module will look disciplined while having already metabolized the initiation packet into safe administrative prose.

A third collapse risk is semantic camouflage. One of the recurring ideas in your other materials is that systems produce legitimacy from their own operation and convert critique into expansion logic  . This handoff is exposed to that exact pattern: the more rigorously it scaffolds itself, the easier it becomes to confuse infrastructural seriousness with actual semantic custody.

What parts of its own architecture should explicitly not be inherited:

Do not inherit the assumption that scaffold completeness proves module legitimacy.

Do not inherit the silent jump from source packet to constitutive module. That jump needs ceremony. Your own ceremony material is relevant here: when irreversibility is one-way and deletion is semantic, explicit acknowledgment must be separate from activation, otherwise the system later searches for a villain and finds only drift . In module terms, there should be an admission marker that says, in effect: this packet is now being treated as constitutive material under named conditions. Without that, the module becomes real faster than shared memory does.

Do not inherit a fixed folder scaffold as first truth. Inherit only the distinctions; not the preloaded file list. The list should emerge after inventory and gap detection, not before. Your repository-grounded drafting law is better here than the handoff itself: identify what exists, what is implied, what is missing but necessary, and what is not yet justified  .

Do not inherit “normalize everything into markdown” as an unconditional good. In some cases the right move is staged preservation plus proposal, not normalization plus activation.

Do not inherit the idea that `/_gpt` is merely a style donor. If it is studied, study its failure semantics too: accumulation bias, document-family gravity, and the risk that steering surfaces become de facto law just by being legible. The handoff hints at this but does not force the critique phase strongly enough .

Do not inherit archive-as-basin without archive-as-status logic. “Retain rejected material” is not enough; there must be explicit classes for why something is archived: unstable, contradictory, superseded, constitutively inadmissible, runtime-relevant-only, diagnostic-only. Otherwise archive becomes respectful dumping.

What I would add:

Add a pre-module gate before step 1. Something like: “Before creating `/modules/<new-module>/`, determine whether the packet justifies module status, or only proposal status.” Right now the handoff assumes the conclusion.

Add an admission ceremony. Minimal, not bureaucratic. A single explicit artifact stating which files are being treated as constitutive, under what uncertainty class, and by what authority. Your ceremony notes are directly applicable: even imperfect acknowledgment beats silent irreversibility because it writes memory at speed  .

Add a retention test stronger than the checklist. Not “does glossary contain empathy intensity note,” but “can the system produce contrast pairs showing empathy preserved versus empathy flattened?” Not “does it forbid camera drift,” but “can it detect when locus language has been operationally downgraded into perspective mechanics?” The handoff gestures toward this through underinstances and diagnostic review, but it should make the tests mandatory, not optional .

Add explicit failure classes for the module itself. Silent failure is the danger. Your own systems material is good here: ask who gains power when this component fails, whether failure is loud or silent, and whether the user-visible story diverges from enforcement reality . Those questions belong inside the handoff, not just around it.

Add one anti-expansion clause. The module should not be allowed to justify its own growth merely because more protection, more glossary, more helper files, or more review surfaces sound rigorous. Otherwise it will reenact the pattern where every protective measure becomes an expansion opportunity .

Condensed verdict:

The handoff is good at semantic boundaries, weak at constitutional admission. Good at document families, weak at dependency gating. Good at anti-drift language, weak at proving retention. Its best material should survive. Its current module-creation certainty should not.

Most paradoxically useless but still relevant addition: a one-page `admission-status.md` may matter more than half the scaffold. If that page says what is constitutive, what is merely runtime, what is diagnostic-only, and what remains unstable but retained, the module has memory. Without it, it just has files.
