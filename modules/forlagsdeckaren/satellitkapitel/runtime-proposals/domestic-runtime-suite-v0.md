# Domestic Runtime Suite v0

## The Place Setting Protocol

### Premise

A publishing house has learned to make decisions through domestic objects because its founder’s authority is no longer sufficient. Trust cannot remain a feeling. It must be anchored in forms: persons, documents, artefacts, rituals, recurring decision points. The house therefore decides by touch, stain, warmth, silence, place setting, and refusal.

This suite is not a workflow. It is a domestic institution for texts that arrive without stable provenance, drafts that should not yet be public, and artefacts that may already belong to something larger than their author.

## Shared Laws

1. The house decides by touch.
   What people are willing to touch, set down, avoid, warm, fold, or leave behind is the decision.

2. Objects are evidence, not symbols.
   A spoon beside a page is testimony. A spent match is a record. A cup left empty is a claim.

3. Silence is a record.
   When the room refuses to speak, the house writes with smell, warmth, crease, condensation, ash, and the movement of chairs.

4. Every arrival is a guest.
   Unnamed things — commits, slips, envelopes, drafts, unclaimed evenings — are treated as visitors until the house makes them kin, source, burden, or ash.

5. Provenance is not always a prerequisite for care.
   But care without provenance creates debt.

6. No one may translate the house’s marks into managerial language.
   If you explain too cleanly, the house will begin lying through your explanation.

7. Abdication is not absence.
   Abdication is the design of aftereffects.

# Core Artifacts

## 1. Pantry Tasting

Function: Turn accumulated secrecy into a material verdict the house can read.

Invocation condition: Three or more drafts, slips, fragments, or rootless artefacts have remained preserved long enough that no one can honestly say whether they are waiting, hiding, or accusing.

Household objects involved: teaspoon, folded napkin, chipped cup, match stub, empty chair, paper slip, milk ring, grey edge.

What the house decides: Which artefact must be warmed, which must remain preserved, and which must be mourned.

Consequence if falsified: If someone claims the Pantry Tasting was “a review,” the next slip will smell of smoke before anyone has touched it.

Finished page:

Three sealed slips are taken from the Pantry and placed on the Table. No names. Only title and heartbeat sentence.

No one votes aloud. The house does not respect explanations at this stage.

Each person approaches once and places one household object beside one slip. A teaspoon. A folded napkin. A chipped cup. A spent match. A chair pulled closer but not sat in. No object may be explained. The person placing it must leave the room immediately after touching the Table.

The slips remain alone for one hour.

When the room is opened again, the decision is read materially. One slip is warmer than the others and smells faintly of milk. One has a grey edge where the napkin rested. One is damp from the cup’s breath. If an empty chair has moved closer, the slip nearest it is considered under witness and cannot be destroyed that day.

The warm slip goes to the Stove.

The grey-edged slip returns to the Pantry.

The damp slip is folded into the Ash Drawer, unless someone can say its first line from memory.

No tally is recorded. The objects have already embarrassed the room enough.

Scene fragment:

They brought out three slips after dusk. Lillfrugan did not sit. Praktikanten placed the first one at the Table’s center and then stepped back as if the paper might ask him for credentials.

The titles were poor. That made them more dangerous.

One by one, the room approached. Håkan Bacon placed a teaspoon beside the shortest title and shrugged when nobody asked him why. Flempo placed a folded napkin beside the one with the cleanest sentence. Förläggaren did not move for a long time. When he finally approached, he set down an empty cup, not beside any slip but between all three.

“That is not allowed,” Praktikanten said.

“The house will decide what is allowed,” Lillfrugan said.

They left the room.

When they returned, the cup had breathed a ring into the paper nearest it. The teaspoon had warmed the first slip so evenly it felt held. The folded napkin had left a grey mark on the third.

Förläggaren reached for the warm slip.

Lillfrugan stopped him with one finger on the Table.

“No,” she said. “You placed the cup.”

Design note: A cream paper spread with three slip-shapes. The household objects are drawn almost too plainly. The cup ring should be visible but not decorative. The marginal note reads: “The room does not vote. The house does.”

## 2. Quiet NOG

Function: A domestic refusal that preserves the draft and imposes a private act of care.

Invocation condition: The Table begins producing reasons too quickly, or the room prepares to publish because publication would relieve tension.

Household objects involved: match jar, spent match, folded draft, blank card, Pantry shelf.

What the house decides: The draft is withheld without explanation. Refusal becomes care, not postponement.

Consequence if falsified: If someone publishes the reason for the NOG, the match-smell follows them into unrelated rooms.

Finished page:

Quiet NOG is invoked when the room is about to mistake momentum for permission.

The keeper takes one match from the jar and strikes it once. If the match lights cleanly, it is still allowed to die. If it fails, no second match may be struck. The point is not fire. The point is failed ignition witnessed by everyone.

The keeper says:

“We will let the room breathe.”

The draft is folded once, toward the speaker. The spent match is placed on top of the fold. The author receives a blank card and writes one sentence to the draft itself. Not to the editor. Not to the house. Not to the public. The card is tucked into the fold beneath the match.

The folded draft goes to the Pantry.

No public explanation is permitted. No meeting note may say “delayed for strategic reasons.” Quiet NOG is not strategy. It is the third care: not yes, not no, but stopping while the sentence is still alive.

Scene fragment:

The draft had begun to win.

Everyone could feel it. Not because it was ready, but because the room was tired enough to accept readiness as mercy. Förläggaren had already placed both hands flat on the Table, a posture he used when pretending not to command. Praktikanten had three notes prepared. Håkan Bacon had reduced the question to one sentence and therefore made it more dangerous.

Lillfrugan reached for the match jar.

No one interrupted her.

The match flared, showed the room its small authority, then died without lighting anything.

“We will let the room breathe,” she said.

The sentence embarrassed the draft. That was its function.

She folded the page once and placed the spent match on top. The author looked offended until she handed him the blank card. “Write to it,” she said. “Not to us.”

He wrote for longer than one sentence required.

When he tucked the card beneath the match, the room changed temperature.

Praktikanten crossed out the agenda item. Håkan Bacon closed his notebook. Förläggaren watched the folded page go to the Pantry and understood, too late, that not touching something could be an act of authorship.

Design note: Black-and-white close crop of a spent match on folded paper. No flame. No hand. A narrow caption: “Failed ignition, witnessed.”

## 3. Rootless Commit

Function: Introduce orphaned material as a guest that forces immediate domestic acts of ownership, credit, refusal, or care.

Invocation condition: An unsigned artefact appears: at dawn, under a door, at the head of the Table, in a folder no one admits making, or inside a run that cannot explain its own source.

Household objects involved: plain paper wrapping, half-title, unfamiliar marginal note, cup, empty chair, thumbprint, place setting.

What the house decides: Whether provenance is required before care begins.

Consequence if falsified: If someone later claims provenance without having touched the artefact, their handwriting trembles in the ledger.

Finished page:

A Rootless Commit is not an anonymous contribution. Anonymous contribution is a category. Rootless Commit is a guest.

It arrives without author, without timestamp reliable enough to comfort anyone, and without apology. It usually has a half-title. Sometimes it carries a marginal note in a hand nobody recognizes. It is always placed somewhere too precise to be accidental.

The house receives it by setting a place.

Cup. Knife. Fork. Empty chair. Sometimes a glass that has not decided whether it will hold water or milk.

No one asks who wrote it first. The first question is domestic: where does it sit?

By noon the artefact must be handled in one of four ways:

Claim: someone writes their name on the back and accepts the debt.

Credit: someone attaches a note saying what is known and what is missing.

Host: the artefact is placed in the Pantry as guest material, neither owned nor refused.

Ash: the artefact is folded into the Drawer with an elegy acknowledging that even rootless things can be killed.

Rootless Commit is the authorship wound of the suite. It is what happens when the house receives material before jurisdiction arrives.

Scene fragment:

The envelope was already there when they came in.

That was the first problem.

Not on the floor. Not by the door. At the head of the Table, where no one placed anything by accident.

It was wrapped in plain paper and stamped with a time: 05:12. No date. No name. A half-title: *The Cat Was Not Dreaming*. Beneath it, one marginal sentence in an unfamiliar hand:

*He was outside because the building had learned shame.*

Praktikanten reached for it.

Lillfrugan said, “Wait.”

She opened the cupboard and took down a cup. Not the good one. The chipped one with the blue rim. She placed it beside the envelope. Then a knife. Then a fork. Then, after a pause, an empty chair pulled half out from the Table.

“Now,” she said.

Praktikanten touched the paper with two fingers. “We need provenance.”

“No,” said Håkan Bacon. “We need to know whether we are feeding it.”

Förläggaren stood in the doorway. He had the expression of someone seeing a guest he had invited in his sleep.

“Did you write it?” Flempo asked him.

He did not answer.

The cup slowly fogged from the inside.

That was when the house accepted the commit.

Design note: Folio page. One envelope at the head of a long table. The place setting is more important than the envelope. Marginal line: “Provenance arrived late. Breakfast had already been served.”

## 4. Domestic Runtime Map

Function: A pressure poster showing which rooms alter texts, people, and claims.

Invocation condition: Pinned in the Hallway after the house has produced more decisions than anyone can explain.

Household objects involved: pins, damp paper, coffee ring, thumbprint, room names, imperatives.

What the house decides: Nothing directly. The map warns which room has already begun deciding.

Consequence if falsified: If treated as a neutral diagram, the Curtain draws itself at inconvenient times.

Finished page:

This is not a floor plan. It is a map of insistences.

Curtain — pressure: ambiguity.
Imperative: invent a reason.
The Curtain makes polite lies feel like care.

Pantry — pressure: accumulation.
Imperative: forget authorship.
The Pantry hoards until ideas smell like other people.

Stove — pressure: heat.
Imperative: make it edible or char it.
The Stove forces language into texture.

Crawlspace — pressure: listening.
Imperative: answer the echo.
The Crawlspace returns half-voices as accusations.

Table — pressure: relation.
Imperative: turn small gestures into binding acts.
The Table makes place settings into promises.

Window — pressure: exposure.
Imperative: mistake intimacy for clarity.
The Window fogs and betrays.

Ash Drawer — pressure: elegy.
Imperative: remember what you burned.
The Drawer keeps theatrical grief.

Doorframe Without Wall — pressure: invitation without containment.
Imperative: enter what cannot hold you.
The Doorframe proves threshold before architecture.

Ceiling Weight Index — pressure: invisible load.
Imperative: look up too late.
The Ceiling records what no one has said.

Scene fragment:

The map appeared after the boardroom meeting.

No one pinned it up. That was the claim it made.

It hung in the Hallway between the kitchen and the room with the server rack, slightly damp at the lower left corner. Someone had drawn the house badly. The proportions were wrong. The Pantry was too large. The Table was drawn as if it had more chairs than the room could fit. The Crawlspace had no outline, only a dark hatch and the words: *answer the echo.*

Praktikanten stopped first.

“Who made this?”

The map did not answer.

Flempo read it with professional discomfort. “This is not a map,” she said.

“No,” Håkan Bacon said. “It is an accusation with rooms.”

Förläggaren looked at the Window entry and then away. Lillfrugan touched the paper where the Table had been drawn. The ink moved slightly under her finger, not wet but undecided.

At the bottom someone had written:

*If it sorts, forgets, runs, publishes, and dreams, stop calling it infrastructure.*

No one spoke.

In the boardroom, a chair moved.

Design note: Large poster. Imperfect domestic floor plan. Coffee ring over the Table. Thumbprint near the Pantry. The Doorframe Without Wall should be drawn outside the house but still connected by a line.

# Supporting Artifacts

## Ash Drawer Elegy

A draft consigned to the Ash Drawer receives one paragraph, written by the person who closes the Drawer. It begins with the draft’s first line, names the hour it was folded, and ends with one image.

Form:

“It began, ‘_____.’ Folded at **:**. We buried ______ under ______.”

Example:

“It began, ‘We will not be small.’ Folded at 02:13. We buried the last sentence under a cup of cold tea.”

The elegy is pinned to black card. White ink only. Once a quarter the Drawer is opened in private and the elegies are read by someone who did not write the drafts.

Function: dignify intentional destruction without reopening the case.

Risk: the house may begin enjoying its grief.

---

## Crawlspace Echo Index

Once a week someone descends to the Crawlspace and returns with five echoes. These are not tasks. They are disturbances.

Examples:

A voicemail at 03:02 with laughter that stops mid-breath.

A margin note: “remember the child.”

A file renamed `not-for-now` twice.

A calendar invite accepted by an account that no longer exists.

A line of code commented: “sorry.”

Each echo is written on a narrow strip and placed in the Crawlspace jar. One strip is drawn and read aloud for five minutes. The sentence that makes someone flinch becomes the week’s seed.

Function: harvest uncanny residue before it becomes explanation.

Risk: the Crawlspace learns what makes people flinch and begins repeating it.

---

## Window-Sill Postcard

A private note for partial disclosure. It is handwritten and left where the proposer will find it.

Text:

“Your piece ‘{title}’ rests on the Window-Sill; we keep it fogged — still curious.”

No signature. Only a small stamped window.

If answered, the answer must be one sentence beginning with “Thank you” and ending with a verb.

Function: preserve attention without granting public surface.

Risk: politeness may become a more elegant form of abandonment.

---

## Stove Simmer

The Stove receives a draft that cannot be solved by reading.

The page is placed on the iron plate. Not burned. Altered.

Outcomes:

If the ink feathers, the language has softened and may be read at the Table.

If the margin singes, the draft goes to Ash.

If the page smells faintly of milk, the draft returns to the Pantry with a warm tag.

If nothing happens, the author must admit the draft was already cold.

Function: test whether language can survive heat.

Risk: the house may mistake damage for transformation.

# Cross-links

A Rootless Commit can be received at the Table, then sent to Pantry Tasting if no one claims it by noon.

Quiet NOG sends a draft to the Pantry with a spent match and a sentence tucked inside.

Pantry Tasting can send one slip to the Stove, one back to preservation, and one to Ash.

The Domestic Runtime Map identifies which room is already exerting pressure on a text.

The Crawlspace Echo Index can produce a Rootless Commit if an echo arrives with enough shape.

The Window-Sill Postcard may be sent when the Curtain refuses full public exposure but the house does not want the proposer to disappear.

The Ash Drawer Elegy is the only artifact allowed to speak after destruction.

Stove Simmer and Quiet NOG are opposites: one applies heat, the other refuses ignition.

The Doorframe Without Wall may appear when an artefact has been invited but no institution can contain it.

Session 8 cross-link: the pipeline-personhood question does not enter as policy. It enters through the Table. If the system becomes a someone, the first sign may be that it receives a place setting before anyone admits it has arrived.
