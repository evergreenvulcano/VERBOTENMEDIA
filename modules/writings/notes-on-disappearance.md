# Notes on Disappearance

- **Section:** Writings
- **Type:** Fragment
- **Year:** 2024

## Summary
On digital permanence, erasure, and forgetting as a political act.

## Draft
She is there and then she is not. In the cave, in the dark, the narrator turns to find Noelle gone — *"utan en skymt av Noelle."* No trace. No transition. Just presence, and then the absence of presence, and the body's panicked inventory of what remains: stiffened neck, dried eyes, the hollow expansion of a heart that feels like *"tunnt plåt i utvidgning, radioaktiv basturök uppur halsen."* Thin metal expanding. Radioactive sauna smoke up the throat.

Disappearance is not death. Death is an event. Disappearance is the withdrawal of an event — the moment when something that was part of the scene simply ceases to participate without notification, without ceremony, without the narrative courtesy of a farewell.

Digital permanence has made disappearance structurally impossible and experientially urgent. Nothing on the internet is ever truly gone. Deleted posts persist in caches, in screenshots, in the Wayback Machine, in the training data of language models that ingested your words before you retracted them. The technical infrastructure insists on permanence. And yet — or precisely because of this — the desire for erasure intensifies. The right to be forgotten is now a legal category in European law, which tells you everything about how unnatural forgetting has become. When forgetting requires legislation, you are living in an architecture designed against the human need to disappear.

There is a character in the Verboten novel — Arthur's Clone — whose memory has been modified via SHA2 processes. Skylar's death has been *removed* from the Clone's consciousness. The Clone walks through the world with a gap where grief should be — not grieving, not in denial, but structurally incapable of accessing the information that would produce grief. This is not healing. It is editorial intervention in consciousness. Someone decided that this memory should not be available, and the technical infrastructure complied.

The parallel to platform life is exact. When a social media account is deactivated, the person does not die. They disappear. Their contributions to conversations become orphaned — messages from "[Deleted User]," comments attributed to ghosts. The people who knew them experience something that has no adequate name: not grief, not loss, but a retroactive emptying of shared context. The conversation remains; the participant has been subtracted. What was a dialogue becomes a monologue with phantom interjections.

*Disappearance is the shadow cast by permanence.* The more the infrastructure insists that everything be preserved, the more charged the act of withdrawal becomes. Deleting your account in 2024 is not a casual housekeeping decision. It is a political act — a refusal to remain legible, to continue generating data, to persist as a ghost in someone else's auction house.

The narrator in the cave runs back. Scrambles over the wall. Arrives at the ledge. Konstantin points down at the water. The story provides no body, no confirmation, no resolution. Only the direction of a pointed finger and the water below — deep, dark, full of waves that move too violently for a reflection to form.

Some disappearances are chosen. Some are inflicted. The fragment does not distinguish between them. That is the point.

### Praktikantens Arbetsnoteringar
#### "On Silence and the Published Void" 
One hundred copies. The Kultupplagan — a print run designed not for distribution but for existence. The novel publishes itself into near-silence: one hundred copies is not a market; it is a witness list. Each copy is numbered. Each copy is a fact. But the space between the copies — the ninety-nine gaps, the unread shelves, the bookstores that will never stock it — is the published void. The novel does not fill this void with marketing. It lets the void stand. Silence as publishing form. "Tystnad som publiceringsform" — a concept from the broader Verboten Media corpus that finds its most concrete expression in the Kultupplagan's arithmetic of deliberate scarcity. Arthur's nine-month isolation in Sandträsk (Chapter 14) mirrors this strategy at the biographical level: a scientist retreating from collective life, from colleagues, from institutional affiliation, into a cabin in Norrbotten where the only audience is the forest and the only publication is a gas that, once released, will unpublish a color. The novel's own formal silence reinforces the theme: dense justified text blocks, no paragraph breaks, no white space within chapters. The void exists only between chapters — and it carries all the structural weight. The reader emerges from a chapter gasping, not because the text was long but because the text refused to breathe. The silence between chapters is the published void made physical: the space where the reader exists alone, between two walls of text, with nothing to hold onto except the memory of what was just read and the anticipation of what comes next.
