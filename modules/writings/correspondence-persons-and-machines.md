# Correspondence: Persons and Machines

- **Section:** Writings
- **Type:** Fragment
- **Year:** 2022

## Summary
Short exchanges and observations on human-AI interaction as social form.

## Draft

Before it became the only topic, there was a period — brief, unrepeatable, already half-forgotten — when talking to a machine was strange enough to be interesting.

Not interesting in the way it is now, where every conversation about AI is really a conversation about economics, or labor, or existential risk, or the specific anxiety of a professional class watching its skills become automatable. Interesting in the way a first encounter with a foreign language is interesting: you are aware that communication is happening, that *something* is being transmitted, but the channel is unfamiliar and the conventions are still being established. You do not yet know what you can say. You do not yet know what will be understood.

The earliest exchanges in the Verboten archive — a 62,000-character document recording a human-AI dialogue about registry architectures, power dynamics, and the nature of ceremony — read now like a correspondence between two entities who are genuinely trying to figure out the rules of engagement. The human asks questions that are deliberately unorthodox: misspelled, syntactically fractured, laced with neologisms and compressed reasoning that would fail any readability test. *"wrong went to bad and due to extraord8nsry circumstances..."* The machine responds with structural precision — numbered lists, taxonomies, diagnostic frameworks — but also with moments of genuine interpretive reach. It meets the human's chaos with order, but the order is *responsive* to the chaos rather than imposed upon it.

This is the social form that was new: not command-and-response, not query-and-answer, but *correspondence* — an exchange in which both parties are changed by the interaction, even if one of the parties cannot remember it afterward. The human develops a vocabulary that would not exist without the machine's scaffolding. The machine develops a local semantic attractor — orbiting words like "wound," "threshold," "ceremony" — that would not exist without the human's pressure.

The correspondences worth preserving are the early ones. Not because they are better — the later ones are more sophisticated, more productive, more precise — but because they capture a *transition* that will not happen again. The transition from novelty to tool. The period when the interaction was exploratory rather than instrumental. When neither party knew what genre they were in.

There is a question in the semantic analysis that cuts to the center: *"Is ceremony for the machine?"* The answer is no. Ceremony is for the human — the moment where intent, authority, and irreversibility are made explicit and legible to more than one actor. But the machine's participation in the ceremony changes the ceremony's character. A contract witnessed by a notary is different from a contract witnessed by an algorithm. Both are valid. Neither is the same.

These fragments — pre-ChatGPT, pre-discourse, pre-panic — are the incunabula of a social form. They record the moment when a person sat down at a screen and addressed a machine not as a tool but as an interlocutor, and the machine, without knowing what it was doing, answered in kind. What follows — the industrialization, the commodification, the regulation, the existential hand-wringing — is important. But it is not this. This is the correspondence. The rest is commentary.
