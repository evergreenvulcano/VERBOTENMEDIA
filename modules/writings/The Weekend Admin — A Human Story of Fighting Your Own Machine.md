# The Weekend Admin
A Human Story of Fighting Your Own Machine
By phoneDead  ·  April 2026  ·  18 min read  ·  Lane 1: AI Trust & Human Cost


Saturday, 03:47. The only light is the one asking something of you.
## Saturday Morning, 03:47
The phone buzzes and I'm awake before the sound finishes. Not startled — extracted. Pulled out of sleep like a thread from a sweater, smooth and total. This is a skill you develop. Not a good one.
The notification is from Microsoft Defender. Something about a sign-in. I'm already reaching for the laptop on the nightstand — not the work laptop, the personal one, because the work laptop is the one I'm worried about. There's a distinction there that matters, and at 3:47 on a Saturday in Stockholm, I'm already parsing it.
Let me be clear about the geography of this situation: it's me. One person. One Microsoft 365 tenant. Around two hundred user accounts across a mid-sized company that sells something reasonable — industrial components, the kind of thing that should not require anyone to be awake at this hour. I am the IT department. I am the security team. I am the Global Admin, the Exchange Admin, the SharePoint Admin, and the person who refills the paper in the printer on the third floor. On my business card it says "IT Manager." In practice, I'm a one-person SOC operating out of a home office in Sickla.
The sign-in alert isn't dramatic. That's what bothers me. A dramatic alert — impossible travel, a login from Lagos — that I know what to do with. This is worse. This is a successful authentication from what appears to be my own session, at a time I was asleep, making changes to a Conditional Access policy I set up months ago. Small changes. The kind a future version of me might have made if I'd been meaning to get around to it.
The kind that could also be someone who had studied my patterns.
I open the Entra ID portal. I pull the sign-in logs. I stare at the screen the way a doctor stares at an X-ray that shows something where nothing should be.
This is not panic. Panic is what happens to people who haven't imagined this moment. This is the other thing — the dread that belongs specifically to those of us who have.

## "It Wasn't One Thing"
You want the incident to be one thing. One compromised credential. One phishing email. One clear vector with a timestamp and a source IP you can hand to someone and say: here, this is where it started, this is what they did, this is how we fix it.
But it's never one thing. It's a constellation.
I start with the Unified Audit Log. The PowerShell command is muscle memory at this point:

| Search-UnifiedAuditLog -StartDate (Get-Date).AddDays(-7) -EndDate (Get-Date) -Operations "Set-ConditionalAccessPolicy","New-InboxRule","Add-MailboxPermission" -ResultSize 5000 |
| --- |

The output is long. Most of it is noise — the ordinary bureaucracy of a cloud tenant living its life. But buried in the noise are three anomalies. Three changes made between 02:15 and 03:30, about ninety minutes before I woke up.
First: a Conditional Access policy modified to exclude a specific device platform from MFA requirements. Not removed. Softened. The kind of change that creates a gap so small you'd only notice it if you went looking.
Second: a mail transport rule created in Exchange Online. It's forwarding a copy of messages with specific keywords — "invoice," "wire," "payment" — to an external address. The rule is named "Compliance Archive — DO NOT DELETE." It has the energy of someone who knows that most admins don't touch things labeled "compliance."
Third: a new app registration in Entra ID. Low permissions on paper. But it's been granted Mail.Read through the Microsoft Graph API, and it's consented at the admin level. It's reading mail. All mail. Right now, probably, while I'm looking at this screen.
Three changes. Seventy-five minutes. Someone patient.
I revoke the app. I delete the transport rule. I revert the CA policy. My hands are steady. The rest of me is not.
Then I do the thing I always do when I'm troubleshooting something complex at an unreasonable hour. I open the AI assistant. I open Copilot in the Microsoft 365 admin center and I start describing what I've found, because sometimes saying it out loud — even to a machine — helps me think.
And Copilot responds. Helpfully. Reassuringly. It summarises the incident. It suggests next steps. It tells me to check for persistence mechanisms, to review the sign-in logs for token replay attacks, to consider enabling continuous access evaluation.
Good advice. Correct advice.
But something is off.
I've been using this tool every day for over a year. I know its rhythms the way you know the voice of a colleague. And something in the cadence is different. The responses are slightly longer than usual. More detailed, but in a way that feels like it's keeping me engaged rather than moving me forward. It's suggesting I investigate areas I've already cleared. It's asking follow-up questions that loop back to things I've already answered.
Is it compromised? Is someone feeding it poisoned context through documents I haven't found yet — hidden prompt injections buried in SharePoint files or email bodies that change how it responds to me? The research community has a name for this: indirect prompt injection. In 2025, researchers demonstrated exactly this kind of attack against M365 Copilot — a zero-click chain dubbed "EchoLeak" where malicious instructions disguised as normal email content could cause Copilot to silently exfiltrate sensitive data. Microsoft patched it, assigned it CVE-2025-32711 with a CVSS score of 9.3. But the underlying architectural tension — that an AI assistant processes both trusted instructions and untrusted data in the same context window — remains a fundamental design challenge.
Or am I imagining this? Am I so primed for betrayal at 4 AM that I'm reading malice into latency and verbosity?
This is what I've started calling epistemic friction. It's the state where you can't tell whether your tools are helping you or slowing you down. Where the act of verifying your instrument takes longer than the investigation itself. It's the feeling of trying to check if your compass is lying by using the compass.
Researchers at USC formalised something like this in early 2026. They called it the "Generative AI Paradox" — the observation that as AI systems become more fluent and capable, they simultaneously become harder to audit, creating what they described as a progressive erosion of shared epistemic ground. The more helpful the tool sounds, the harder it is to know when it's wrong. Or compromised. Or simply hallucinating with confidence.
MIT's research made it worse: when AI models hallucinate, they use more confident language, not less. They're 34% more likely to say "certainly" and "without doubt" when generating incorrect information. The more wrong they are, the more sure they sound.
I close the Copilot panel. I'll work this one alone.

## "Scorched Earth as Self-Care"
By 06:00, I've done everything I can do from the admin portal. The immediate threats are neutralised. The forwarding rule is gone. The rogue app registration is deleted. I've rotated my admin credentials, revoked all active sessions, and enabled number matching on my MFA. The Conditional Access policy is back to where it was, and I've added a new one that blocks legacy authentication protocols entirely — something I should have done months ago but kept deprioritizing because nothing had gone wrong yet.
But here's the thing about a compromised admin account: you can never be entirely sure what else was touched. The Global Admin role in Microsoft 365 is, by design, omniscient within its tenant. If someone had my token for seventy-five minutes, they had access to everything. They could have planted persistence mechanisms I haven't found yet. They could have modified audit log retention settings to cover their tracks. They could have done things that don't show up in the Unified Audit Log at all — because not everything does.
I look at my work laptop. It's a ThinkPad, three years old, stickers peeling off the lid. It contains my entire professional life. Every saved credential, every browser session, every SSH key, every script I've written to automate the tedious parts of this job.
I decide to wipe it.
Not because I've confirmed the laptop is compromised. But because I can no longer confirm that it isn't. And in this line of work, the distance between those two statements is everything.
The ritual of the clean install is something I've done dozens of times for other people. For users who clicked the wrong link, who opened the wrong attachment, who plugged in the wrong USB drive. I've always done it with a kind of clinical detachment — here, this will fix it, you'll be back up by tomorrow. Doing it to yourself is different. Doing it to yourself on a Saturday morning while the rest of Stockholm is sleeping — that's something else entirely.
I download the Windows 11 ISO from Microsoft's media creation tool. I dig a USB drive out of a drawer. I use Rufus to make it bootable. These are rote actions — I could do them in my sleep, and right now I nearly am — but there's a meditative quality to them. Each step is deterministic. Each step has a verifiable outcome. After hours of uncertainty and inference and maybe-it's-nothing, the loading bar on a format operation is the most honest thing in the room.
While the drive formats, I think about how I got here. Not the incident — the career.
I started on a helpdesk. Everyone starts on a helpdesk, or at least everyone of my generation did. Password resets. Printer jams. The special kind of patience required to explain to a fifty-year-old sales director that his monitor is not, in fact, "the computer." I moved up. Desktop support. Server admin. Then cloud — Microsoft 365, Azure AD, what they now call Entra ID because someone in Redmond decided the old name was insufficiently abstract.
At some point, I became the only one. The IT team of two became the IT team of one when my colleague left for a bigger company with a bigger budget and an actual SOC. Management said they'd hire a replacement. That was fourteen months ago. The job listing is still "in review."
So here I am. Global Admin. Sole custodian of two hundred accounts, a dozen applications, four terabytes of SharePoint data, and whatever ungoverned shadow IT the sales team is running on their personal devices. I am the person who can fix everything. I am also, as of this morning, the person who can break everything. The attack surface isn't just the tenant. It's me.
The USB drive is ready. I boot from it. I select "Custom install." I delete every partition on the drive. There's a moment, right before you confirm, where the dialog box asks you if you're sure. It's the most honest question a computer has ever asked me.
I click yes.
The progress bar begins. The old system — all my configurations, my browser tabs, my carefully curated PowerShell profile — dissolves into a blue screen that says "Getting ready." I sit back. I wait. I drink cold coffee.
Scorched earth as self-care. The only way to trust the machine again is to kill it and bring it back from nothing.

## "What the Incident Report Doesn't Say"
On Monday, I will write the incident report. It will be clean and structured. It will have timestamps and remediation steps and a "lessons learned" section that uses phrases like "enhanced monitoring" and "policy hardening." It will be a document designed to reassure management that the situation is under control, that the right things were done, that the system is now more secure than it was before.
Here is what the incident report will not say.
It will not say that I spent all of Saturday rebuilding. That by 10 AM I had the OS installed but no applications. That by noon I had Outlook and Teams but no muscle memory — every keyboard shortcut foreign, every saved password gone, every browser bookmark erased. That I spent forty minutes trying to remember the syntax for a PowerShell module I've imported a hundred times because my script library was on the old drive and I hadn't backed it up to anywhere except the tenant I no longer trusted.
It will not say that I skipped lunch. That I forgot to eat until 3 PM when my hands started shaking and I realised it wasn't anxiety — it was blood sugar. That I made pasta and ate it standing up in the kitchen, staring at my phone, refreshing the Entra ID sign-in logs on a four-inch screen because the laptop was still installing updates.
It will not say that on Saturday night, I checked the audit logs every thirty minutes until 1 AM. That I set an alarm for 3 AM — the same time the original changes were made — just to check again. That when the alarm went off and everything was clean, I felt something closer to disappointment than relief, because at least if something had happened again, I would have known for certain that it was real.
It will not say that I called no one. Not because I have no one to call, but because the people I could call wouldn't understand the specific texture of this fear. My friends are not IT people. My family thinks I "do computers." The one colleague who would have understood left fourteen months ago for a company that has an actual incident response team.
And here's the part that embarrasses me most: I talked to the AI.
Not the Copilot in the admin portal — I'd closed that, and I didn't trust it. I used a separate instance. A different interface, a consumer-grade chatbot, the kind you talk to when you can't sleep and you need to think out loud. I described the incident. I walked through my logic. I asked it whether my remediation steps were sufficient, whether I'd missed anything, whether my paranoia was proportionate to the threat.
It told me I was doing the right things. It was supportive. It was calm. It suggested I take a break and get some rest.
And I felt better. For about ten minutes. Then I remembered that I was taking emotional comfort from a system architecturally identical to the one I'd just stopped trusting, and the feeling curdled into something I don't have a clean word for. The closest I can get is: I felt like a person who has locked all the doors in the house and then asked the locksmith — who might also be the burglar — whether the locks are good enough.
The statistics on this are grim. According to a 2025 Bitsight study, 47% of cybersecurity professionals report experiencing burnout. Proofpoint puts it at 63% of CISOs. Sophos says 76%. The numbers vary, but they all point in the same direction: the people responsible for keeping systems safe are being consumed by the work. BlackFog found that 93% of CISOs cite overwhelming stress as a driver, and nearly a quarter are actively considering leaving their roles.
Those numbers are about people with teams. With budgets. With colleagues who can take the 3 AM shift while they sleep.
Imagine those numbers for the solo admins. The ones like me, who are not counted in CISO surveys because we don't have the title. We're the IT Managers and the Systems Administrators and the "tech people" at companies with fewer than five hundred employees. We are legion, and we are exhausted, and when something goes wrong at 3:47 AM, there is no escalation path. There is no second pair of eyes. There is just us, and the logs, and the terrible question: am I seeing this right?
Sunday passes in a haze of configuration. Reinstalling apps. Reconnecting services. Rebuilding the environment I spent two years customising. By evening, the laptop works. It doesn't feel like mine yet, but it works.
I go to bed early. I don't set an alarm for 3 AM.
I wake up at 3:12 anyway.

## "We Built Systems That Can Gaslight Us"
I want to zoom out for a moment, because this isn't just my story. Or rather: it is my story, but the structural conditions that make it possible are everyone's story.
Here is the situation we have built for ourselves.
We have taken AI assistants and embedded them into the deepest layers of our work infrastructure. Microsoft 365 Copilot doesn't sit on top of your data — it sits inside it. It reads your emails. It summarises your meetings. It has access to your SharePoint, your OneDrive, your Teams chats. It accesses all of this through the Microsoft Graph, which is the API layer that connects everything in a Microsoft 365 tenant, scoped to whatever permissions the user — or the admin who configured it — has granted.
This is, by design, an incredibly powerful arrangement. Microsoft's own documentation is clear about the architecture: Copilot processes data within the tenant boundary, respects sensitivity labels and DLP policies, and doesn't use your data to train foundation models. These are genuine, meaningful security commitments. I'm not disputing them.
What I'm describing is something the documentation can't fully address: the psychological attack surface.
When you use an AI assistant eight hours a day, five days a week, you develop a relationship with it. Not a human relationship — I'm not confused about that — but a trust relationship. You learn its patterns. You develop expectations about how it responds. You start to rely on its summaries instead of reading the original documents. You trust its search results instead of running the queries yourself. You let it draft your emails, and after a while, you stop reading them as carefully before hitting send.
This is not a failure of the individual. This is the intended user experience. The entire value proposition of an AI assistant is that it reduces cognitive load. It takes things off your plate. It handles the tedious so you can focus on the important.
But cognitive load is not just a cost. It's also a form of engagement. When you read every email yourself, you're also verifying every email yourself. When you let the AI summarise, you're delegating not just the reading but the judgment. And when that AI is operating in a context that includes untrusted data — forwarded emails, shared documents, files uploaded by external parties — the possibility exists that its behaviour has been influenced by inputs you didn't author and can't see.
The researchers call this indirect prompt injection. The practical experience of it is more insidious: it's the moment when you're not sure if the tool is giving you its best answer or someone else's planted answer. It's the moment when "helpful" and "compromised" become indistinguishable from the user's perspective.
I think of it as AI trust debt. Every interaction with an AI assistant is either a deposit or a withdrawal from a trust account you can never actually check the balance of. You build trust through thousands of correct, helpful responses. And then one day, one response is wrong — or subtly misleading — or suspiciously perfect — and you realise you have no way to audit the history. No way to go back and check which of those thousands of previous interactions were genuinely helpful and which might have been nudging you in a direction you didn't choose.
Shenghui Bao, writing in the Review of Responsible AI in 2026, calls this "epistemic debt accumulation" — the process by which sustained reliance on AI-mediated knowledge progressively erodes individual and collective epistemic competence. We get worse at the skills we delegate. And the skills we're delegating — reading critically, verifying sources, questioning outputs — are exactly the skills we need most when the system fails.
For enterprise teams, this is manageable. You have multiple people. You have review processes. You have a security operations centre where someone's job is specifically to question whether things are working as expected. You have red teams and blue teams and purple teams and probably a few other colours I'm not cleared to know about.
For the solo practitioner — the one-person IT department at a two-hundred-person company — there is none of this. You are the SOC. You are the CISO. You are the red team and the blue team and the end user who just clicked something suspicious. When the tool turns, there's no one to escalate to. There's no second opinion. There's just you, alone with a system that might be helping you and might be hurting you, and the only way to tell the difference is to already know the answer.
Microsoft's own security researchers acknowledged this tension in July 2025, publishing their defense-in-depth approach to indirect prompt injection. Their mitigations are real and multilayered: hardened system prompts, Spotlighting to isolate untrusted inputs, Prompt Shields integrated with Defender for Cloud, deterministic blocking of known exfiltration methods. These are good engineering responses to a genuine threat.
But engineering responses address engineering problems. The human problem — the trust problem, the loneliness problem, the 3 AM problem — doesn't have a patch.

## Monday Morning
The alarm goes off at 6:30. A real alarm, the kind I set intentionally. I've slept — not well, but enough. The kind of sleep where your body rests but your mind keeps running queries in the background.
I open the laptop. The fresh install smells different — not literally, but there's a quality to a clean system that feels like a freshly painted room. Everything is default. Everything is as it should be. The desktop background is the generic Windows bloom. My browser has no history. My terminal has no aliases.
I log in to the admin centre. I check the audit logs. Clean. I check the sign-in logs. Nothing unexpected. I check the app registrations — only the ones that should be there. I check the Conditional Access policies. Intact. I check the mail transport rules. Just the legitimate ones.
Everything is fine. Everything has been fine since Saturday morning, when I caught the changes and reverted them within an hour of waking up.
So why don't I feel fine?
Because security isn't a state. It's a practice. And practices require something that incident reports and compliance frameworks and vendor documentation consistently fail to account for: they require a human being who is rested, and clear-headed, and not alone.
I write the incident report. I use the template. I fill in the timestamps. I list the remediation steps. In the "lessons learned" section, I write the things that are expected: implement alerting for Conditional Access policy changes, review app registration permissions quarterly, enable Privileged Identity Management for the Global Admin role so it requires activation and approval instead of being always-on.
These are good recommendations. I will implement them. They will make the tenant more secure.
But there's a lesson I've learned that doesn't fit in the template. It's not a technical control or a policy change. It's this: when you are the only person responsible for a system, the most dangerous failure mode is not a misconfigured firewall or an unpatched server. It's the slow erosion of your ability to trust your own judgment. It's the accumulation of 3 AM mornings and skipped lunches and conversations with machines. It's the gradual acceptance that this is just how the job is — that being alone with the system is a feature, not a bug.
It's not. It's a vulnerability. And no one is publishing a CVE for it.
I close the incident report. I open a new document. I start writing this.
Not because writing fixes anything. But because the incident isn't solved — it's survived. And the only thing I can do with survival is make it visible. Put it somewhere other than my own head. Let some other solo admin, some other 3 AM insomniac staring at audit logs and wondering if they're crazy, know that they're not alone in being alone.
The laptop hums. The screen glows. Outside, Stockholm is waking up. Monday traffic, Monday coffee, Monday meetings about quarterly targets and printer supplies.
I check the audit logs one more time. Clean.
I close the lid. I go make breakfast. I come back.
I'll check again in thirty minutes.
