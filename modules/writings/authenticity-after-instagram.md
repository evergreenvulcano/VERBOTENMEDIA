## CARD 9 — AUTHENTICITY AFTER INSTAGRAM
**Criticism · 2023**

*"Du skola icke bli ett med din mask. Din mask ska bli ett med dig."* This line from the Verboten manifesto inverts the anxiety that drives most discourse about authenticity online. The conventional worry is that we are becoming our masks — that the performed self, the curated feed, the strategically filtered photograph has replaced the "real" person underneath. The manifesto suggests the opposite trajectory: the mask should become *you*. Not the dissolution of authenticity into performance, but the absorption of performance into identity. The mask does not replace the face. The mask *becomes* the face. And once it does, the distinction between authentic and artificial ceases to operate.

This is the condition Instagram created and could not contain. The platform was designed as a display technology — a grammar for appearing. It provided filters, frames, aspect ratios, and a social reward system (likes, followers, engagement metrics) that collectively constituted a *vocabulary of appearance*. Within this vocabulary, users learned to produce a specific kind of self: aspirational, visually coherent, narratively curated, and — crucially — *recognizable as a brand*. The authentic/artificial distinction was never relevant to this grammar. What mattered was consistency. What mattered was the feed as a unified aesthetic object.

But the cultural discourse could not abandon authenticity. It mutated instead. "Authenticity" became a *genre* — a particular kind of performance characterized by visible imperfection, confessional tone, and the strategic deployment of vulnerability. The "authentic" post is the one that shows the messy kitchen, the crying selfie, the admission of failure. It is no less curated than the aspirational post. It simply operates within a different set of aesthetic conventions. The mess is composed. The tears are well-lit. The failure is narratively resolved within three paragraphs.

The Verboten fragment analysis traces this phenomenon through the mechanism of *parasocial relationship cultivation*: algorithms optimizing for attachment to content creators who model specific behaviors. The "authentic" influencer is a technical artifact — a node in a recommendation system that has learned that vulnerability generates engagement. The algorithm does not distinguish between genuine vulnerability and performed vulnerability. It measures clicks. And clicks are the same regardless of what produced them.

What remains of sincerity as a cultural value? The question is not rhetorical. Sincerity — the condition of meaning what you say and saying what you mean, without strategic calculation of audience response — was never a natural state. It was always a cultural achievement, requiring social contexts that rewarded honesty over performance. Those contexts are becoming rare. Not because people have become less honest, but because the *infrastructure* through which they communicate has been optimized for engagement rather than truth. And engagement, structurally, rewards performance.

After Instagram, authenticity is not dead. It has been *subsumed* — absorbed into the grammar of appearance as one style among many. The sincere person and the performing person are now indistinguishable from the outside. The mask has become the face. Whether this is liberation or catastrophe depends on whether you believe the face was ever anything more than a mask to begin with.
